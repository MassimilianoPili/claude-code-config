## Research Notes

### 1. Comparable Projects Worldwide

**Jardin Japonais de Monaco** (opened 1994)
- Designed by Yasuo Beppu, commissioned by Prince Rainier III
- 7,000 m2, built on reclaimed land near the sea
- Key lessons: heavy irrigation infrastructure required; sea spray is a constant problem;
  uses windbreaks extensively; full-time gardening staff (3-4 people for 7000m2)
- Climate: Csa (hot-summer Mediterranean), very similar to coastal Sardinia but MORE humid
- They maintain a koi pond with recirculating filtered water -- significant infrastructure
- Annual cost reportedly in the hundreds of thousands of euros (municipal budget)
- [VERIFY] Exact staffing and budget numbers

**Huntington Japanese Garden, San Marino/Pasadena CA** (original 1912, expanded 2012)
- ~5 acres (~20,000 m2), one of the oldest Japanese gardens in the US
- Climate: Csa, severe drought risk (California water restrictions)
- Drought adaptations: recycled water for irrigation, drought-tolerant substitute species,
  reduced lawn area, drip irrigation throughout
- They replaced some traditional water features during drought years
- Full professional horticultural staff (part of larger Huntington institution)
- Budget: part of a $50M+ annual institutional budget
- Key lesson: institutional backing makes survival possible during multi-year droughts

**Adelaide Himeji Garden, Australia** (1985)
- ~2,500 m2, sister-city gift from Himeji to Adelaide
- Climate: Csb (warm-summer Mediterranean), quite dry (550mm rain/year)
- Adelaide gets LESS rain than most Sardinian candidate sites
- Maintained by Adelaide City Council parks department
- Uses native Australian gravel in place of some traditional materials
- Adapted plant palette: some Japanese maples replaced with local alternatives
- Key lesson: sister-city relationship provided both design expertise AND ongoing cultural support

**Giardino Giapponese di Roma (Orto Botanico)** [VERIFY exact status]
- Small Japanese garden section within the botanical garden
- Mediterranean climate, managed by La Sapienza university
- Relevant as closest Italian precedent

**Jardim Japones, Madeira (Portugal)** [VERIFY existence and status]
- Monte Palace Tropical Garden has a Japanese-inspired section
- Similar island Mediterranean context

**Israeli examples**:
- Kibbutz Ein Gev and Kibbutz Ramat Rachel have small Japanese-inspired gardens
- Extremely arid -- they rely almost entirely on drip irrigation and drought-adapted species
- Karesansui (dry garden) style is most viable in arid climates

**Morocco**:
- No significant Japanese gardens found in Morocco
- Majorelle Garden (Marrakech) is the closest in spirit but is Moorish/botanical, not Japanese

**Common failure modes across all projects**:
1. **Drought**: #1 killer. Water features dry up, plants die, moss impossible to maintain
2. **Wind**: Damages niwaki (pruned trees), desiccates plants, scatters gravel
3. **Wrong soil pH**: Japanese maples and azaleas need acidic soil (pH 5.0-6.5)
4. **Maintenance gap**: Even 2-3 months without skilled pruning degrades niwaki significantly
5. **Salt spray** (coastal): Kills most Japanese garden plants
6. **Fire**: Not widely reported as a failure mode for gardens specifically (irrigated areas resist fire better than surrounding brush)

### 2. Maintenance Requirements

**Hours per week for 5,000 m2**:
- Peak season (April-October): 20-30 hours/week minimum
- Off-season (November-March): 8-12 hours/week
- This assumes the garden is ESTABLISHED (years 3+). During establishment (years 1-3), double these numbers.

**Can one person maintain it?**
- Marginally, if that person is skilled and it's their primary occupation
- Realistically: 1 full-time skilled gardener + seasonal help (200-300 hours/year extra)
- Japanese gardens in Japan: traditional ratio is ~1 gardener per 1,000-2,000 m2
- For 5,000 m2: 2.5-5 gardeners in Japan; in a simpler Western adaptation, 1-2 can manage

**Seasonal maintenance calendar**:

| Month | Tasks | Hours/week |
|-------|-------|------------|
| Jan-Feb | Dormant pruning (deciduous), pond maintenance, fence/structure repair | 8-10 |
| Mar | Spring cleanup, moss care, pre-season fertilizing, weed prevention | 15-20 |
| Apr-May | Niwaki shaping (pine candle pruning in May), azalea pruning post-bloom, planting | 25-30 |
| Jun-Jul | Irrigation management, weed control, hedge trimming, pest monitoring | 20-25 |
| Aug | Irrigation critical, heat stress management, reduced pruning | 15-20 |
| Sep-Oct | Autumn cleanup, fall pruning, bulb planting, pond prep | 20-25 |
| Nov-Dec | Leaf removal, winter protection (frost covers), tool maintenance | 8-12 |

**Key specialized tasks**:
- **Niwaki pruning**: 2-3 times/year, requires genuine skill (courses in Japan or with Japanese master)
- **Karesansui raking**: weekly during growing season, 2-3 hours per session for a medium-sized area
- **Pond management**: weekly water quality checks, seasonal cleaning (2x/year major), pump/filter maintenance
- **Moss care**: nearly impossible in hot Mediterranean summers without shade + constant moisture

**Annual cost estimate for Italy** (2026 prices):
- 1 full-time gardener (operaio agricolo qualificato): EUR 22,000-28,000 gross/year
- Seasonal help (200 hours x EUR 12-15/hour): EUR 2,400-3,000
- Water (irrigation, 300-500 m3/year if spring-fed is free, otherwise): EUR 500-1,500
- Materials (gravel, fertilizer, replacement plants, tools): EUR 2,000-4,000
- Pond maintenance (chemicals, filter media, fish food): EUR 500-1,000
- Structural maintenance (wood, stone repair): EUR 1,000-2,000
- **Total: EUR 28,000-40,000/year** for a well-maintained 5,000 m2 garden
- This EXCLUDES the owner's own labor. If owner does ~50% of work: EUR 15,000-22,000/year

**Can it survive 2-4 weeks unattended?**
- **Winter (Nov-Feb)**: YES, easily. Deciduous plants dormant, irrigation off, frost risk manageable with covers
- **Autumn (Oct-Nov)**: YES, 2-3 weeks fine. Leaf accumulation but no damage
- **Spring (Mar-May)**: RISKY. 2 weeks OK, 4 weeks and you'll miss critical pruning windows
- **Summer (Jun-Aug)**: NO without automated irrigation. Plants can die in 5-7 days without water in Sardinian heat. With automated drip irrigation + timer: 2-3 weeks possible, 4 weeks risky (pump failure, clog, etc.)

**Mitigation for absences**: automated irrigation with redundant systems (2 independent circuits), moisture sensors, remote monitoring via IoT/camera, and a local contact for emergencies.

### 3. Legal Framework

**3.1 Environmental Impact Assessment (VIA)**
- A private garden on agricultural land does NOT typically require a full VIA (Valutazione di Impatto Ambientale)
- However, if the pond involves:
  - Excavation > 500 m3: may trigger municipal authorization
  - Modifying a natural watercourse: requires nulla osta from Genio Civile
  - Using spring water: requires concessione idrica (see below)
- The key regulation: D.Lgs. 152/2006 (Testo Unico Ambientale), Part II
- A simple ornamental pond (no dam, no river diversion) on private land: municipial SCIA or permesso di costruire should suffice
- [VERIFY] Sardinian regional implementation specifics (LR 3/2008, piano di bacino)

**3.2 Vincolo Paesaggistico**
This is the MOST critical legal issue. D.Lgs. 42/2004 (Codice dei Beni Culturali e del Paesaggio).

**Limbara versante Est (Gallura)**:
- Monte Limbara is classified as area of landscape interest under multiple vincoli:
  - Art. 142, comma 1, lettera d): mountains above 1,200m (Limbara peaks at 1,359m)
  - Art. 142, comma 1, lettera g): forests and woodlands -- heavily forested area
  - The EASTERN slopes specifically: [VERIFY] whether the specific altitude band (600-900m, likely target for property) falls under vincolo
- PPR (Piano Paesaggistico Regionale) della Sardegna: Gallura is classified as Ambito di Paesaggio n. 17 (Gallura costiera interna)
- [VERIFY] Exact PPR classification for inland Gallura/Limbara slopes
- If under vincolo: any construction (including garden structures, ponds, paths) requires Autorizzazione Paesaggistica from Soprintendenza -- adds 3-6 months to permitting

**Bono/Monte Rasu (Goceano)**:
- Monte Rasu area: likely forest vincolo (Art. 142 lettera g)
- SIC/ZPS designations possible (Rete Natura 2000) -- [VERIFY]
- Goceano is Ambito di Paesaggio n. 14 in PPR
- Lower tourist/landscape pressure means Soprintendenza may be MORE flexible
- However, depopulation areas sometimes have stricter conservation rules to prevent land-use change

**Santu Lussurgiu (Montiferru)**:
- Post-2021 fire area: likely under special reforestation vincolo
- Art. 142, comma 1, lettera l): areas damaged by fire -- CANNOT change land use for 15 years from fire date (i.e., until 2036)
- This is a CRITICAL constraint: if the specific parcel was burned, you CANNOT convert it from forestry/agriculture to garden/recreational use until 2036
- [VERIFY] Exact perimeter of the 2021 fire and whether it covers candidate parcels
- Even unburned parcels near fire zone may have enhanced forestry vincolo

**Bottom line**: ALL three sites likely have some form of vincolo paesaggistico. Limbara and Santu Lussurgiu are the most constrained. Bono/Monte Rasu may be the easiest for permitting, paradoxically.

**3.3 Water Rights (Concessione Idrica)**

Italian water law: ALL water (surface and underground) is public property (Art. 822 Codice Civile, D.Lgs. 152/2006).

- **Spring on private land**: You DO need a concessione di derivazione (water abstraction permit) from the Regione Sardegna (Servizio del Genio Civile competente per territorio)
- Small domestic use (uso domestico): simplified procedure, but "domestic" = household use, not irrigation of a 5,000 m2 garden
- For a pond + irrigation: likely classified as "uso irriguo" or "uso ricreativo"
- Permit process: application to Genio Civile, public notice (30 days), technical assessment, concession granted for 15-30 years, renewable
- Annual fee (canone): relatively low for small abstractions (EUR 50-200/year for <1 L/s)
- Maximum abstraction: depends on the specific spring's yield and other users downstream
- There is NO absolute right to "all the water" from a spring on your land
- Timeline: 3-12 months for the permit
- [VERIFY] Sardinian regional regulations: LR 29/2002, regolamento regionale acque

**Key point**: if the spring yield is sufficient (>0.1 L/s = ~8,640 L/day), this is MORE than enough for a 5,000 m2 garden. A typical Japanese garden pond of 100 m3 would need initial filling + evaporation replacement (~5-10 L/day per m2 of open water surface).

### 4. Cultural Project Angle

**4.1 Framing as a Cultural Institution**

A Japanese garden CAN be framed as:
- **Botanical garden** (orto botanico): requires registration with BGCI, scientific purpose, documented collections
- **Cultural institution** (istituto culturale): more flexible, can qualify for cultural funding
- **Art installation / land art**: could fall under contemporary art frameworks
- **Agriturismo with cultural component**: combines hospitality with cultural offering -- this is probably the MOST practical framing for rural Sardinia

Benefits of institutional framing:
- Access to cultural funding (EU, national, regional)
- Tax benefits (IVA reduced, IMU exemption for cultural properties)
- Easier permitting (cultural purpose may override some vincolo restrictions)
- Visitor revenue becomes tax-advantaged

**4.2 Japan-Italy Cultural Exchange Programs**

**Istituto Giapponese di Cultura (Roma)**:
- Part of the Japan Foundation network
- Primarily focused on: exhibitions, film, language courses, lectures

### 6. Decision Framework

**If forced to choose today: Limbara versante Est (Gallura)**

Reasoning:
- Granite substrate is the single most irreplaceable advantage -- you cannot create granite landscapes, but you CAN build windbreaks, install irrigation, and amend soil
- Proximity to Olbia (50 min) = realistic access from mainland Italy
- Gallura has better tourism infrastructure = future visitor potential
- Wind is manageable with multi-year windbreak strategy
- Vincolo paesaggistico is a risk but NOT a blocker (garden is compatible with landscape protection if done well)

**Minimum Viable Version (MVV)**

The smallest/cheapest version that still qualifies as a Japanese garden:

**Area**: 1,000-1,500 m2 (not 5,000 -- start small, expand later)

**Elements**:
1. **Karesansui** (dry rock garden): 200 m2 -- lowest maintenance, most drought-resistant, showcases granite
2. **Niwaki area**: 5-8 trees, focus on Pinus pinea (native, trainable) rather than imported Japanese species
3. **Tsukubai** (stone water basin) with recirculating pump -- symbolic water, minimal actual water use
4. **Gravel paths** with stepping stones (tobi-ishi)
5. **Simple bamboo fence** (take-gaki) enclosure
6. NO pond in phase 1 (saves EUR 15,000-30,000 and avoids water permit complexity)
7. NO tea house in phase 1 (saves EUR 20,000-50,000)

**MVV Budget estimate**:
- Land (1 hectare agricultural, inland Sardinia): EUR 15,000-40,000
- Site preparation (clearing, grading, drainage): EUR 5,000-10,000
- Rocks and gravel (local granite): EUR 3,000-8,000
- Plants (8-10 specimen trees, 50-100 shrubs/groundcover): EUR 3,000-6,000
- Irrigation system (automated drip): EUR 2,000-4,000
- Paths, fencing, tsukubai: EUR 3,000-6,000
- Tools and equipment: EUR 1,000-2,000
- **Total MVV: EUR 32,000-76,000**

**Phase 2** (years 3-5): Add pond (100-200 m2), expand planted area to 3,000 m2
**Phase 3** (years 5-10): Tea house or meditation pavilion, expand to full 5,000 m2

This phased approach:
- Tests the site before major investment
- Lets windbreaks mature before planting sensitive species
- Spreads cost over 5-10 years
- Allows learning and course-correction

## Serendipitous Connections

**Wabi-sabi and information theory**: The aesthetic of imperfection in Japanese gardens (wabi-sabi) has a formal connection to Kolmogorov complexity -- the most aesthetically valued arrangements are those that appear natural (high apparent complexity) while being generated by simple principles (low algorithmic complexity). This is the same tension between compression and expressiveness that appears in information theory and ML model design.

**Bradley-Terry and garden design**: The Ranking Todo project's preference-learning approach could be applied to garden element placement. Japanese garden design is traditionally taught through ranked comparisons ("this stone placement is better than that one"), which is exactly the pairwise comparison structure that Bradley-Terry models.

**Sardinian granite and crystallography**: The granitic batholiths of Gallura (Hercynian orogeny, ~300 Ma) produce the same weathering patterns (tafoni) that Japanese garden designers prize as "naturally aged" rock. There's a geological reason why Sardinian granite is an excellent substitute for Japanese garden stones -- both are Paleozoic granites with similar mineral compositions.
