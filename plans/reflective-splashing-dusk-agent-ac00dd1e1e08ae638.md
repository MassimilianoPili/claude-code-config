# Fauna of a Japanese Garden with Pond at 600-800m in Sardinia (Acidic Substrate)

## Research Summary

**Context**: Japanese garden with pond, 600-800m altitude, acidic substrate (granitic/schistose), likely in Goceano or similar central-northern Sardinian mountain area. The vegetation context implies cork oak (Quercus suber), holm oak (Q. ilex), chestnut (Castanea sativa), and Mediterranean maquis.

**Epistemic status**: Mixed -- amphibian and butterfly data are well-documented in peer-reviewed literature; firefly, odonata, and mycorrhizal data for Sardinia specifically are more sparse, drawing on general Mediterranean ecology and T7 sources (Wikipedia, naturalist databases). Legal questions draw on Italian regulatory framework knowledge.

---

## 1. Amphibians for the Pond

Sardinia has a remarkably small but highly endemic amphibian fauna -- only 7 native species, of which 3 are Corso-Sardinian endemics.

### Hyla sarda (Sardinian/Tyrrhenian Tree Frog)

- **Status**: Corso-Sardinian endemic (also Tuscan Archipelago). IUCN Least Concern. (T7 -- Wikipedia/IUCN)
- **Habitat**: Temperate forests, shrubland, rivers, freshwater marshes, intermittent marshes, even urban areas. Very adaptable.
- **Altitude range**: Sea level to over 1,200m. Fully within 600-800m range.
- **Pond colonization**: **Highly likely**. This species readily colonizes garden ponds, cattle troughs, cisterns, and any standing water. It breeds in still or slow-moving water with emergent vegetation.
- **Requirements**: Shallow margins with reeds/rushes/iris for egg-laying (eggs are deposited on submerged vegetation). Needs some vegetation around the pond edge for calling perches.
- **Call**: Males produce a distinctive repeated "cra-cra-cra" call, somewhat harsher and slower than the common European tree frog (H. arborea). Calling occurs primarily at dusk and through the night, April-June. The chorus of multiple males is one of the most characteristic sounds of Sardinian wetlands.
- **Legal status**: Protected under Bern Convention (Appendix II), EU Habitats Directive (Annex IV). It is **illegal to capture or disturb**, but creating habitat to attract them is perfectly legal and encouraged.

**Verdict**: This will almost certainly colonize a garden pond on its own within 1-3 years if there are populations nearby. No intervention needed -- just build the pond with proper margins.

### Discoglossus sardus (Tyrrhenian Painted Frog)

- **Status**: Corso-Sardinian endemic (also Tuscan Archipelago islands). IUCN Least Concern. (T7 -- Wikipedia/IUCN)
- **Habitat**: Temperate forests, rivers, intermittent rivers, freshwater marshes. Found from sea level to over 1,700m.
- **Pond requirements**: Prefers slow-moving water or temporary pools. Can tolerate slight water pollution and even brackish water. Breeds in shallow water; eggs laid singly or in small groups on the bottom (not on vegetation like Hyla).
- **Description**: Robust build, 5-7.5 cm. Dark brown to grey/reddish brown with lighter spots. Distinctive reverse-droplet pupil shape.
- **Altitude**: Well within the 600-800m range. Present throughout Sardinia except central highlands (but this may reflect survey gaps rather than true absence).
- **Legal status**: Habitats Directive Annex II and IV. Fully protected in both Italy and France. **Strong conservation species** -- creating habitat is actively encouraged.
- **Threats**: Chytridiomycosis (Bd fungus), habitat loss from urbanization.

**Verdict**: Good candidate for natural colonization. Prefers more temporary/seasonal water features -- a shallow overflow area or seasonal bog garden would especially attract this species.

### Euproctus platycephalus (Sardinian Brook Newt/Salamander)

- **Status**: **Sardinian endemic only** (not even in Corsica -- the Corsican species is E. montanus). IUCN **Endangered** (previously Critically Endangered). The rarest and most threatened salamander in Europe. (T1 -- Lecis & Norris, Biological Conservation 2004; Bovero et al., Herpetological Bulletin 2005; Tessa et al., Amphibia-Reptilia 2023)
- **Altitude range**: 50-1,800m, with most sightings in the middle range. 600-800m is within optimal range.
- **Habitat**: Cold, clean, slow-moving mountain streams and small pools. Hides under stones. Requires **cold water temperatures** -- this is the key limiting factor.
- **Pond suitability**: **Unlikely for a garden pond**. This species requires running water (streams), cold temperatures (around 10-15C), and very clean conditions. A still garden pond would likely be too warm in summer, especially at 600-800m. However, if the garden includes a stream-fed feature with cold, flowing water and rocky substrate, there is a possibility.
- **Threats**: Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis (Bd chytrid fungus) -- documented since 2006 in Limbara populations (T1 -- Tessa et al. 2023). Also: water extraction, introduced trout (Salmo trutta), pollution, dam construction.
- **Population genetics**: Three genetically divergent subpopulations (north/Limbara, central/Gennargentu, south/Sette Fratelli), isolated since Pleistocene glacial oscillations ~0.5-1 Mya. (T1 -- Ball et al., Ecology and Evolution 2017)
- **Legal status**: IUCN Endangered. Bern Convention. Habitats Directive Annex IV. Regional Law 23/1998 (Sardinia). **Strictly protected -- do not attempt to capture or relocate.**

**Verdict**: Do NOT expect this in a garden pond. If the property has a natural cold mountain stream, the newt might already be present -- survey before any modification. Any stream-associated construction should be designed to avoid disturbing potential habitat.

### Other Native Amphibians at 600-800m

- **Bufo viridis (European green toad)** -- previously B. viridis balearicus, now sometimes classified as Bufotes viridis or B. balearicus. Present throughout Sardinia, sea level to ~1,000m. Could colonize a garden pond, especially one with more open surroundings. Nocturnal, musical trilling call.
- **Bufo bufo spinosus (Common toad, Sardinian population)** -- present at these altitudes. Large, terrestrial except during breeding. Would use the pond for spring breeding (February-April).
- **Speleomantes spp. (Cave salamanders)** -- 3 endemic species in Sardinia (S. supramontis, S. imperialis, S. sarrabusensis). Terrestrial, lungless, found in caves and moist rock faces. Would NOT use the pond but might inhabit stone walls and rock gardens in the Japanese garden.

### Creating Habitat -- Legal Framework

Creating a garden pond is **perfectly legal** and is considered habitat creation/enhancement. Under Italian law (DPR 357/1997 implementing the Habitats Directive), you cannot:
- Capture, kill, or disturb protected species
- Destroy breeding sites or resting places

But you CAN:
- Create new aquatic habitat (ponds, bog gardens)
- Plant native vegetation to attract wildlife
- Let species colonize naturally

This is actively encouraged by conservation bodies. No permit needed for a private garden pond in Italy, though very large water features (>dam-like structures altering watercourses) would need authorization from the Genio Civile.

## 2. Dragonflies and Damselflies (Odonata)

### Species Expected at 600-800m Mountain Ponds in Sardinia

Sardinia hosts approximately 50-55 Odonata species. At 600-800m with a garden pond, the following are most likely:

**Dragonflies (Anisoptera):**
- **Anax imperator** (Emperor dragonfly) -- large, blue, common at ponds up to 1,000m. One of the first colonizers of new ponds.
- **Libellula depressa** (Broad-bodied chaser) -- blue/brown, very common, one of the first to colonize new ponds in Mediterranean areas.
- **Orthetrum cancellatum** (Black-tailed skimmer) -- common at open ponds.
- **Orthetrum coerulescens** (Keeled skimmer) -- prefers slightly acidic/heathland ponds. Excellent match for acidic substrate.
- **Sympetrum striolatum** (Common darter) -- very common late-summer dragonfly.
- **Sympetrum fonscolombii** (Red-veined darter) -- migrant, common in Mediterranean.
- **Crocothemis erythraea** (Scarlet darter) -- bright red, increasingly common with warming climate.
- **Aeshna mixta** (Migrant hawker) -- autumn species, patrols pond margins.

**Damselflies (Zygoptera):**
- **Ischnura genei** -- **Corso-Sardinian endemic damselfly**. A highlight species. Common at still waters on both islands.
- **Coenagrion puella** (Azure damselfly) -- classic pond damselfly.
- **Ceriagrion tenellum** (Small red damselfly) -- prefers slightly acidic boggy margins. Good match.
- **Calopteryx haemorrhoidalis** (Copper demoiselle) -- streams and pond outflows. Mediterranean species.
- **Lestes barbarus** / **L. virens** -- spreadwings, breed in temporary and permanent ponds.

### Pond Features to Attract Odonata

1. **Shallow margins** (10-30 cm) -- essential for most species' larvae and for emergence.
2. **Emergent vegetation**: Iris pseudacorus, Typha latifolia, Juncus spp. -- for egg-laying and larval habitat.
3. **Open sunlit water surface** -- dragonflies are heliothermic; at least 50-70% of the pond surface should receive direct sun for most of the day.
4. **Submerged vegetation**: Provides larval hunting grounds. Myriophyllum, Potamogeton, or even Ceratophyllum.
5. **Varied depth**: Shallow margins graduating to 60-100 cm depth. Deeper zones protect overwintering larvae.
6. **No fish** (or very few) -- fish are the main predators of dragonfly larvae. A fish-free pond will have dramatically more Odonata than one with koi. **This creates a direct conflict with the koi plan (Section 6).**
7. **Flat stones at water's edge** -- basking sites for adult dragonflies.
8. **Nearby tall vegetation/trees** -- shelter from wind, perching sites for hawkers.

## 3. Fireflies (Lucciole)

### Species in Sardinia

- **Lampyris noctiluca** (Common glowworm) -- the most widespread European firefly. Present throughout Mediterranean Europe including Sardinia. Range extends from Portugal to China, and almost to the Arctic Circle. (T7 -- Wikipedia)
  - **Not a true "flying firefly"**: Females are wingless and larviform, emitting a steady yellowish-green glow from their last abdominal segments. Males are winged with brown elytra but glow faintly.
  - **Peak display**: June-July, during warm evenings, for about 2 hours after dusk.
  - Males can detect female glow from up to 45m away.

- **Luciola italica** (Italian firefly) -- the classic Mediterranean flying firefly with pulsing flash. Present in mainland Italy and Sicily. **Presence in Sardinia is uncertain/disputed** -- some records exist but it may be confused with other Lampyridae. If present, this would be the species that provides the classic "floating lights" display.

- **Luciola lusitanica** -- another possible species in western Mediterranean, but Sardinian records need confirmation.

- **Phosphaenus hemipterus** (Lesser glowworm) -- very rare, primarily northern European. Unlikely at this latitude.

### Habitat Requirements for Fireflies at 600-800m

1. **Moisture**: Firefly larvae are ground-dwelling predators of snails and slugs. They need damp soil, leaf litter, and ground cover. A Japanese garden with moss patches, leaf litter under trees, and a pond creating local humidity is excellent.
2. **Leaf litter and ground layer**: Undisturbed areas with leaf litter where larvae hunt for 2-3 years before pupating. Do NOT rake all leaf litter -- leave areas of natural accumulation.
3. **Darkness**: This is **critical**. Fireflies cannot compete with artificial light. At 600-800m in rural Sardinia, light pollution should be minimal -- a huge advantage.
   - No external lighting near the garden at night (or use only red/amber wavelength lights at very low intensity, shielded downward).
   - Sardinian mountain areas are among the darkest in Italy, especially in the interior.
4. **No pesticides**: Insecticides and molluscicides (snail bait) directly kill firefly larvae. A Japanese garden maintained organically is ideal.
5. **Grassland and meadow margins**: Adult females display in low grass where they can be visible to flying males. Maintain some unmown grass areas.

### When to Expect Them

- **Season**: June-July, peak around the summer solstice.
- **Time**: From about 30 minutes after sunset until 23:00-midnight.
- **Temperature**: Activity increases with warm, still, humid evenings. Above 15C minimum.

**Verdict**: Lampyris noctiluca is highly probable if the garden is maintained organically with good leaf litter and darkness. The display is more subtle than tropical fireflies (stationary glow rather than flying flashes), but atmospheric and beautiful, especially reflected in a pond.

## 4. Birds

### Songbirds at 600-800m in Sardinian Woodland

The bird community at this altitude in Sardinia is a mix of Mediterranean endemics/specialists and widespread European woodland species. Expected residents:

**Year-round residents:**
- **Curruca melanocephala (Sardinian warbler)** -- despite its name, this is NOT a Sardinian endemic. It is a common Mediterranean species (genus recently moved from Sylvia to Curruca). The name comes from Francesco Cetti's description in "Gli uccelli di Sardegna" (1776). Male: black cap, grey back, white throat, red eye-ring. Song: fast rattling. Common in maquis and gardens at all altitudes. (T7 -- Wikipedia)
  - **Note on "Sylvia sarda"**: This was the former name for the **Marmora's warbler** (now Curruca sarda), which IS a genuine Corso-Sardinian breeding endemic (also found in Balearics and small N. African areas). It prefers low maquis, especially on hillsides with Cistus, Erica, and low scrub. At 600-800m it would be present if the garden borders open maquis.
- **Parus major** (Great tit) -- very common, will use nest boxes.
- **Cyanistes caeruleus** (Blue tit) -- common woodland species.
- **Periparus ater** (Coal tit) -- present at these altitudes in conifer/mixed woodland.
- **Sitta europaea** (Nuthatch) -- present in mature oak/chestnut woodland.
- **Certhia brachydactyla** (Short-toed treecreeper) -- on tree trunks.
- **Regulus ignicapilla** (Firecrest) -- in dense vegetation, year-round.
- **Fringilla coelebs** (Chaffinch) -- one of the most common woodland birds. Sardinian population is resident.
- **Serinus serinus** (Serin) -- small yellow finch, very vocal.
- **Carduelis carduelis** (Goldfinch) -- attracted by thistles, seed heads.
- **Turdus merula** (Blackbird) -- common in gardens.
- **Erithacus rubecula** (Robin) -- resident in Sardinia (unlike northern populations that migrate).
- **Garrulus glandarius ichnusae** (Jay, Sardinian subspecies) -- endemic subspecies, common in oak woodland.
- **Corvus corax** (Raven) -- common in mountains.
- **Columba palumbus** (Woodpigeon) -- common.
- **Dendrocopos major** (Great spotted woodpecker) -- in mature trees.
- **Alectoris barbara** (Barbary partridge) -- replaces the red-legged partridge in Sardinia. Ground-dwelling.

**Summer visitors:**
- **Upupa epops** (Hoopoe) -- spectacular, insectivorous, nests in tree holes.
- **Cuculus canorus** (Cuckoo) -- iconic spring sound.
- **Various warblers**: Phylloscopus spp. on passage.

### Pond-attracted Birds

- **Alcedo atthis (Kingfisher)**: **Possible but unlikely**. Kingfishers need fish-stocked water and steep banks for nesting. A koi pond *might* attract occasional visits, but they are not common breeders at 600-800m in Sardinia.
- **Ardea cinerea (Grey heron)**: **Will visit** if koi are present. They are common throughout Sardinia and will find any pond with fish within a few km radius. This is actually a potential **problem** for koi (see Section 6).
- **Gallinula chloropus (Moorhen)**: Needs larger water bodies with dense marginal vegetation. Unlikely for a garden-scale pond unless it is quite large (>50 m2 with dense reeds).
- **Motacilla cinerea (Grey wagtail)** and **M. alba (White wagtail)**: Very likely visitors to pond margins, especially the grey wagtail which likes rocky streams and pond edges.

### Plants to Attract Birds

**Berry-bearing shrubs for winter birds:**
- **Arbutus unedo** (Strawberry tree) -- native, acidophilic, fruits in autumn/winter. Attracts Sylvia warblers, blackbirds.
- **Myrtus communis** (Myrtle) -- native, berries in autumn.
- **Viburnum tinus** (Laurustinus) -- berries through winter.
- **Pistacia lentiscus** (Mastic/Lentisc) -- small berries, common in maquis.
- **Juniperus oxycedrus** (Prickly juniper) -- berries attract thrushes.
- **Rosa canina** (Dog rose) -- rosehips in winter.
- **Rubus spp.** (Brambles) -- if managed, excellent for birds and butterflies.

**Plants for insectivorous birds:**
- Dense shrub layers with varied structure (different heights).
- Herb-rich ground layer attracting insects.
- Dead wood piles -- attract beetles and grubs for woodpeckers.
- Flowering plants for pollinators (which in turn attract insectivorous birds).

## 5. Butterflies

### Native Butterflies at 600-800m on Acidic Substrate

Sardinia has about 55 butterfly species including several endemics:

**Sardinian/Corso-Sardinian endemics:**
- **Papilio hospiton** (Corsican/Sardinian swallowtail) -- **the flagship species**.
  - Wingspan 72-76mm. Black and yellow, shorter tails than P. machaon.
  - Habitat: **Mountainous regions 400-1,500m** -- perfectly within the 600-800m range.
  - Host plants: **Umbelliferae** (Apiaceae): Ferula communis (giant fennel), Ruta corsica (Corsican rue), fennel (Foeniculum vulgare), Peucedanum paniculatum.
  - **Bivoltine**: Two broods -- May and August.
  - Threats: Habitat destruction, disappearance of food plants, excessive collecting.
  - Legal status: **CITES Appendix II**, Habitats Directive Annex II and IV. Strictly protected. (T7 -- Wikipedia)
  - **Garden relevance**: Planting giant fennel (Ferula communis) and wild fennel (Foeniculum vulgare) in the garden could attract this butterfly for egg-laying. This would be a significant conservation contribution.

- **Coenonympha corinna** (Corsican heath) -- Corso-Sardinian endemic. Small, yellowish-red. Locally very plentiful May-June and July onwards. Larvae feed on grasses (Carex, Triticum). (T7 -- Wikipedia)

- **Maniola nurag** (Sardinian meadow brown) -- **Sardinian endemic**. Brown butterfly of rocky grasslands. Present at these altitudes.

- **Pseudophilotes barbagiae** -- endemic blue butterfly of central Sardinian mountains.

**Common Mediterranean species likely present:**
- **Papilio machaon** (Swallowtail) -- common, similar to P. hospiton but more widespread.
- **Gonepteryx cleopatra** (Cleopatra) -- striking yellow/orange, associated with Rhamnus.
- **Charaxes jasius** (Two-tailed pasha) -- large, impressive, larvae on Arbutus unedo (strawberry tree).
- **Vanessa atalanta** (Red admiral), **V. cardui** (Painted lady) -- common migrants.
- **Pieris spp.** (Whites) -- common garden butterflies.
- **Lycaena spp.** (Coppers), **Polyommatus spp.** (Blues) -- grassland butterflies.

### Butterfly Host Plants Native to Sardinia

| Butterfly | Host Plant | Native? | Acidic soil? |
|-----------|-----------|---------|-------------|
| Papilio hospiton | Ferula communis, Foeniculum vulgare, Ruta corsica | Yes | Yes (adaptable) |
| Papilio machaon | Foeniculum vulgare, Daucus carota | Yes | Yes |
| Charaxes jasius | Arbutus unedo | Yes | Excellent (calcifuge) |
| Gonepteryx cleopatra | Rhamnus alaternus | Yes | Tolerates acid |
| Coenonympha corinna | Carex spp., grasses | Yes | Yes |

### Garden Plants to Attract Butterflies

**Nectar sources (adult feeding):**
- Lavandula stoechas (French lavender) -- native, loves acidic soil
- Rosmarinus officinalis (Rosemary) -- native
- Thymus herba-barona (Sardinian thyme) -- endemic
- Mentha spp. (Mints) -- native along streams
- Origanum vulgare (Oregano)
- Centranthus ruber (Red valerian)
- Buddleja davidii (Butterfly bush) -- non-native but extremely effective. Use with caution (can be invasive).
- Sedum spp. -- late-season nectar for autumn butterflies

**Japanese garden compatible nectar plants:**
- Wisteria (limited butterfly value but architectural)
- Iris (native I. pseudacorus for wet areas)
- Prunus species (spring nectar when flowering)

## 6. Fish for the Pond

### Koi (Cyprinus carpio) -- Legal Status in Italy

- **Legal status**: Koi are a variety of common carp (Cyprinus carpio), which is classified as an **allochthonous/invasive species** in Sardinia. Under Italian law (DPR 120/2003, implementing EU Regulation 1143/2014 on invasive alien species), the introduction of non-native species into the wild is prohibited.
- **Private pond exception**: Keeping koi in a **contained private pond** with no connection to natural watercourses is generally **permitted without specific permits** in Italy. The key legal requirement is that the pond must be **completely isolated** from any natural water system -- no overflow to streams, no flood risk that could release fish.
- **Sardinian specifics**: Sardinia has historically been very protective of its freshwater ecosystems (which have many endemic species). The regional regulations (Legge Regionale) may impose additional restrictions. It is advisable to check with the local Corpo Forestale (now Corpo forestale e di vigilanza ambientale della Sardegna) before stocking.
- **Practical note**: At 600-800m, winter water temperatures in an outdoor pond may drop to 2-5C. Koi survive this if the pond is deep enough (minimum 120-150cm depth to prevent complete freezing, though full freezing is very unlikely at this altitude in Sardinia).

### Native Sardinian Freshwater Fish

Sardinia's native freshwater fish fauna is extremely limited:
- **No truly native freshwater fish** in inland waters. Sardinia's rivers were historically fish-poor or fish-free due to its island isolation.
- Most current freshwater fish are **introduced**: Salmo trutta (brown trout, widely stocked since ~1900), Anguilla anguilla (European eel, naturally present in coastal rivers), Mugil spp. (mullets, in lower river reaches).
- **Gasterosteus aculeatus** (Three-spined stickleback) may be native to some Sardinian rivers.

There are **no suitable native Sardinian freshwater fish for a garden pond**.

### Mosquito Control

- **Gambusia holbrooki** (Eastern mosquitofish) -- widely used but is itself an **invasive alien species** in Europe. Its introduction into natural waters is illegal under EU regulations. In a private, isolated pond it is *technically* feasible but ecologically problematic -- Gambusia are voracious predators that devastate aquatic invertebrate communities, including dragonfly larvae.
- **Better alternatives**:
  - **Koi themselves** will eat mosquito larvae effectively.
  - **Pond design**: Moving water (fountain, waterfall) prevents mosquito breeding in those areas. Mosquitoes need still water.
  - **Dragonfly larvae**: A thriving Odonata population (see Section 2) provides excellent biological mosquito control -- but this requires a fish-free or low-fish pond.
  - **Bacillus thuringiensis israelensis (Bti)** -- biological larvicide, safe for other wildlife. Available as granules or dunks. Legal and widely used in Italy.
  - **Encourage bats**: Bat boxes near the pond. Bats are voracious mosquito predators.

### The Koi vs. Wildlife Dilemma

This is the critical design tension: **a koi pond and a wildlife pond are fundamentally different ecosystems**.

| Feature | Koi Pond | Wildlife Pond |
|---------|----------|---------------|
| Fish | Yes (koi) | No or very few small fish |
| Dragonflies | Few (larvae eaten) | Abundant |
| Amphibians | Reduced (eggs/tadpoles eaten) | Abundant |
| Aquatic plants | Limited (koi eat/uproot) | Rich |
| Water clarity | Managed (filtration) | Naturally clear via plants |
| Mosquito control | Koi eat larvae | Dragonfly larvae eat larvae |
| Heron risk | High | Low |

**Recommendation**: Consider a **two-pond design** -- a main koi pond (deeper, filtered, more architectural/Japanese) and a separate wildlife pond or bog garden (shallower, planted, no fish). Connected only visually, not hydraulically. This is a common solution in Japanese-inspired gardens that want both aesthetic koi and ecological richness.

## 7. Problematic Wildlife

### Cinghiale Sardo (Sus scrofa meridionalis -- Sardinian Wild Boar)

- **Subspecies**: The Sardinian wild boar is smaller than mainland European wild boar (adults 60-80 kg vs. 100-200+ kg). Sometimes classified as Sus scrofa meridionalis.
- **Presence at 600-800m**: **Absolutely yes.** Wild boar are the most problematic large mammal in Sardinia. They are abundant throughout the island, especially in forested mountain areas like Goceano.
- **Garden damage**: **Severe**. Wild boar will:
  - Root up lawns, moss gardens, and plantings searching for bulbs, grubs, and earthworms
  - Destroy pond margins (they love muddy edges)
  - Eat fallen fruit
  - Damage low walls and paths by rooting alongside them
  - Visit at night (crepuscular/nocturnal)
- **A Japanese garden with its moss, carefully raked gravel, and pond is essentially a magnet for wild boar.**

**Protection strategies:**
1. **Fencing** -- the ONLY reliable solution:
   - **Minimum height**: 120-150 cm (Sardinian boar are smaller, but can push hard)
   - **Type**: Welded mesh (maglia saldata) with gauge of at least 5mm wire, mesh size no larger than 10x10 cm at the base
   - **Ground barrier**: Either bury the mesh 30-40 cm underground, or bend it outward at ground level (L-shape, 50cm apron) to prevent digging under. Sardinian boar are persistent diggers.
   - **Electric fencing**: Very effective as either a standalone 2-wire electric fence or as a supplement to mesh. Two wires at 25 cm and 50 cm height, 5,000-7,000V pulsed. Solar-powered units work well at this altitude.
   - **Traditional Sardinian solution**: Dry stone walls 120-150 cm high work if well-maintained, but boar can breach gaps.
   - **Aesthetic option**: Combine a lower stone wall (80 cm, Japanese style) with 2 electric wires above it. Or use a ha-ha (sunken wall/ditch) for invisible fencing.
2. **Gate**: Must be equally robust. Self-closing gates with boar-proof latches.
3. **No feeding attractants**: Do not compost food waste openly. Secure rubbish bins.

### Cervo sardo (Cervus elaphus corsicanus -- Sardinian Deer)

- **Status**: Endemic subspecies. Was critically endangered but now recovering and expanding, especially in Goceano forests (reintroduction programs since 1985 in Corsica, already present in Sardinia). (T7 -- it.Wikipedia)
- **Presence at 600-800m**: Yes, particularly in Goceano, Arci-Grighine, and Settefratelli.
- **Garden damage**: Moderate to severe. Deer browse on:
  - Young tree shoots and bark (Acer palmatum would be a prime target!)
  - Roses, hostas, and many ornamental plants
  - They do NOT root like boar, so ground-level damage is less
- **Protection**: The same perimeter fencing that stops boar will generally stop deer if it is 150 cm+ high. Individual tree guards (wire mesh cylinders) protect vulnerable specimens like Japanese maples. Deer netting on young plantings.

### Istrice (Hystrix cristata -- Crested Porcupine)

- **Status**: Present throughout Sardinia. Native to Italy (unlike much of northern Europe where it was introduced). Protected species.
- **Garden damage**: Moderate. Porcupines:
  - Gnaw on bark, especially in winter (can ring-bark and kill young trees)
  - Dig for bulbs and roots (less destructive than boar)
  - Eat vegetables and fruit
- **Protection**: Perimeter fencing that stops boar also stops porcupines. Individual tree guards for valuable specimens (wire mesh at base, 60 cm high).
- **Note**: Porcupines are nocturnal and largely harmless. Their quills are impressive but they are not aggressive.

### Snakes at 600-800m

**No venomous snakes in Sardinia.** Sardinia and Corsica are famously lacking in vipers. The only viper-like snake is:

- **Natrix helvetica cetti** (Sardinian grass snake) -- endemic subspecies of the barred grass snake. **Endangered** and declining. Non-venomous, semi-aquatic, feeds on amphibians. Positively correlated with altitude and precipitation; present mainly in eastern Sardinia. (T1 -- Di Nicola et al., Life 2023) A garden pond could actually benefit this rare snake -- it would be a positive conservation outcome.

- **Natrix maura** (Viperine snake) -- non-venomous water snake, often confused with vipers due to its defensive posture. Present in Sardinia, semi-aquatic.

- **Hierophis viridiflavus** (Western whip snake) -- large (up to 180 cm), fast, non-venomous. Very common. Eats rodents, lizards. Beneficial.

- **Hemorrhois hippocrepis** (Horseshoe whip snake) -- present in Sardinia, non-venomous.

- **Chalcides spp.** (Skinks) -- legless or near-legless lizards, not true snakes. Harmless.

**All snakes in Sardinia are non-venomous and legally protected.** They are beneficial garden inhabitants. The pond will attract Natrix species which are themselves conservation priorities.

### Overall Protection Strategy

A single **perimeter fence** solution protects against all large problematic wildlife:
- 150 cm height welded mesh or combination stone wall + electric wire
- 30-40 cm buried or L-shaped ground apron (anti-digging for boar)
- Self-closing gate
- **Estimated cost**: 30-50 EUR/linear meter for mesh fencing professionally installed, more for stone wall + electric hybrid.

This fence also keeps out stray dogs (which are a significant problem in rural Sardinia) and provides a defined garden boundary that is very compatible with Japanese garden design (the concept of "borrowed landscape" or shakkei works well with a boundary).

## 8. Mycorrhizal Fungi

### Ectomycorrhizal Fungi under Quercus suber in Sardinia

Cork oak (Q. suber) hosts a rich ectomycorrhizal (ECM) community. Key species documented in Sardinian sugherete (cork oak forests):

**Edible ECM species associated with Q. suber:**
- **Boletus edulis** complex (porcini) -- YES, documented under Q. suber in Sardinia, though more commonly associated with Castanea sativa and Q. ilex. More reliable in mixed oak-chestnut woodland.
- **Boletus aereus** (dark cep / porcino nero) -- often more common than B. edulis under Mediterranean oaks including Q. suber.
- **Amanita caesarea** (Caesar's mushroom / ovolo buono) -- the "king of mushrooms" in Mediterranean cuisine. Thermophilic, associated with Q. suber and Q. ilex at lower-middle altitudes. 600-800m is at the upper edge but still possible.
- **Cantharellus cibarius** (Chanterelle) -- present in Sardinian oak-chestnut woods on acidic soil. Acidic substrate is actually preferred.
- **Lactarius deliciosus** (Saffron milk cap) -- under pines (Pinus pinaster, P. nigra). If there are pines nearby, very likely.
- **Russula spp.** -- numerous edible species under oaks.

### Acer palmatum (Japanese Maple) and Local Mycorrhizae

- **Compatibility**: Acer palmatum is an ECM/AM dual-capable species. In nature it associates with both ectomycorrhizal and arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi.
- **Local inoculation**: Japanese maples planted in soil near established oaks or chestnuts will likely pick up local ECM fungi naturally. The ECM inoculum is present in forest soil.
- **Specific inoculants**: Commercial mycorrhizal inoculants (containing Rhizophagus irregularis for AM, or Pisolithus tinctorius for ECM) can be applied at planting. Pisolithus tinctorius is naturally present in Sardinian acidic soils and is a generalist ECM fungus that colonizes many tree species.
- **Recommendation**: When planting Acer palmatum, mix local forest soil (from under nearby oaks) into the planting hole. This provides natural ECM inoculum perfectly adapted to the local conditions and acidic substrate.

### Truffle Potential on Acidic Soils

| Species | Common Name | Soil pH | Acidic soil? | Sardinia presence |
|---------|-------------|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| Tuber borchii | Bianchetto truffle | 5.2-8.0 | **YES** -- tolerates down to pH 5.2 | Present throughout, including Corsica. Fruits Dec-June. (T7 -- Wikipedia) |
| Tuber aestivum | Summer truffle (scorzone) | 7.0-8.5 | No -- needs calcareous | Present in Sardinia on limestone areas only |
| Tuber melanosporum | Black truffle (Perigord) | 7.5-8.5 | No -- needs calcareous | Very limited in Sardinia |
| Tuber magnatum | White truffle (Alba) | 7.0-8.5 | No -- needs calcareous | NOT present in Sardinia |

**Tuber borchii is the only truffle with real potential on acidic substrate.** It is remarkably adaptable:
- Grows under oaks, pines, and even in T. melanosporum habitat
- Fruits at 200-1,000m elevation -- the 600-800m range is ideal
- Bears fruit within 4 years in new plantations
- Not extremely host-specific
- Value: 300-400 EUR/kg (not as expensive as T. magnatum but still significant)
- **Sardinia has wild T. borchii populations.** Natural colonization is possible if the soil conditions are right.

**Practical recommendation**: If you want to cultivate T. borchii intentionally, purchase inoculated seedlings of Quercus ilex or Corylus avellana (hazelnut) from a certified nursery (the truffle nurseries in Tuscany or Umbria, e.g., Urbani Tartufi, sell T. borchii-inoculated plants). Plant them in the garden or surroundings. First truffles in 4-7 years.

### Boletus edulis (Porcini) and Chestnuts/Oaks

- **Association**: B. edulis forms ECM with many trees including Quercus spp., Castanea sativa, Fagus sylvatica, Picea, Pinus.
- **Sardinian context**: Porcini are well-known in Sardinian mountain areas, especially in chestnut groves (castagneti) and mixed oak-chestnut forests at 500-1,000m. The 600-800m altitude band is optimal.
- **Acidic substrate**: B. edulis tolerates and often **prefers** acidic soils (pH 4.5-6.5). Acidic granite/schist substrate is ideal.
- **Can you cultivate porcini in a garden?** Very difficult. Unlike truffles, commercial B. edulis inoculation technology is still unreliable (this is why porcini cannot be farmed -- one of the most valuable wild-gathered fungi precisely because cultivation fails). However:
  - **Presence of mature oaks or chestnuts** in or near the garden is the best predictor.
  - **Do not disturb the soil** around established trees -- this preserves the mycelial network.
  - Watering during dry summer months can improve fruiting.
  - B. edulis spores can be spread by making a "spore slurry" (blend old porcini in water, pour around tree roots). Success rate is low but non-zero.

### Shiitake on Chestnut Logs -- the Japanese Connection!

This is a wonderful convergence of the Japanese garden theme and Sardinian ecology:

- **Lentinula edodes** (Shiitake) is a **saprophytic** (not mycorrhizal) fungus that grows on dead hardwood logs.
- **Chestnut (Castanea sativa)** is one of the BEST substrates for shiitake cultivation. Chestnut is abundant in Sardinian mountain forests at 600-800m.
- **Method**: Cut chestnut logs (10-20 cm diameter, ~1m long) in winter when dormant. Drill holes and insert shiitake spawn dowels (available from mushroom cultivation suppliers). Stack in a shaded, humid area (under tree canopy, near the pond where humidity is higher). Soak periodically. First mushrooms in 6-18 months, production continues for 3-5 years per log.
- **Japanese garden aesthetic**: A shiitake log yard (hotagi-ba) is a traditional feature of Japanese mountain settlements. Neatly stacked inoculated logs in a shaded corner of the garden would be both productive and culturally authentic.
- **Climate suitability**: 600-800m in Sardinia has adequate humidity for shiitake, especially if logs are placed near the pond. Summer may require supplementary watering/misting. Winter temperatures (rarely below -5C at this altitude) are fine -- shiitake strains for outdoor cultivation tolerate these ranges.
- **Legal**: No permit needed for cultivating shiitake on private property.

### Other Edible Mushroom Cultivation

- **Pleurotus ostreatus** (Oyster mushroom) -- also grows on chestnut logs or straw. Easier than shiitake.
- **Ganoderma lucidum** (Reishi) -- medicinal mushroom, grows on hardwood logs. Japanese cultural significance (linked to longevity).
- **Hericium erinaceus** (Lion's mane) -- grows on hardwood logs. Excellent edible/medicinal. Can be cultivated alongside shiitake.

## Summary of Key Recommendations

### The Garden Ecosystem as an Integrated Whole

| Element | Expected Natural Colonizers | Active Plantings/Installations |
|---------|---------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Pond** | Hyla sarda, Discoglossus sardus, Bufo spp., dragonflies, Natrix snakes | Native aquatic plants (Iris, Typha, Juncus), submerged vegetation |
| **Woodland edge** | Sardinian warbler, Marmora's warbler, tits, firecrest, robin | Arbutus, myrtle, viburnum, juniper, strawberry tree |
| **Open areas** | Papilio hospiton (!), Coenonympha corinna, blues/coppers | Ferula communis, Foeniculum, Lavandula stoechas, Thymus herba-barona |
| **Leaf litter/ground** | Lampyris noctiluca (fireflies), cave salamanders | Undisturbed leaf litter areas, moss, no pesticides |
| **Trees** | Boletus edulis, chanterelles, Amanita caesarea | Maintain/plant Quercus suber, Q. ilex, Castanea sativa |
| **Log piles** | Native saproxylic fungi | Shiitake, oyster mushroom on chestnut logs |
| **Soil** | Tuber borchii (wild) | T. borchii-inoculated Quercus/Corylus seedlings |
| **Perimeter** | Cinghiale excluded | 150cm fence with buried/L-apron + electric option |

### Conservation Value

A well-designed Japanese garden with pond at this location could provide habitat for:
- 2-3 endemic amphibian species (including the conservation-priority Discoglossus sardus)
- 1 endemic damselfly (Ischnura genei)
- 1 endangered endemic butterfly (Papilio hospiton -- if Ferula/fennel planted)
- 1 endangered endemic snake (Natrix h. cetti -- if near a water source)
- Multiple endemic bird subspecies

This is not a trivial contribution. In a landscape increasingly fragmented by development, fire, and agricultural intensification, private gardens with ecologically designed water features serve as stepping stones and refugia for endemic species.
