# Niwaki for Mediterranean Species in Sardinia -- Research Report

## Executive Summary

Niwaki (Japanese garden tree pruning) is highly compatible with Mediterranean species native to Sardinia. The key insight from the Monaco Japanese Garden -- designed by Yasuo Beppu in 1994 on the Cote d'Azur -- is that Mediterranean plants can be trained into Japanese forms because they share structural similarities with traditional Japanese garden plants: evergreen habit, dense foliage, and responsive branching. This report covers species-by-species suitability, technique, timing, and practical contacts.

**Epistemic status:** Practitioner knowledge (T5-T7 level). Niwaki is an artisan tradition, not an academic field -- there are no peer-reviewed papers. The primary authority is Jake Hobson's book and the RHS guidance. Mediterranean adaptation is documented through practitioner experience in southern France, Monaco, and increasingly in Spain/Italy.

**Confidence:** Medium-High for technique transfer (many practitioners have done it successfully); Medium for specific Sardinian species (limited documented examples, but strong botanical reasoning).

## 1. Niwaki on Mediterranean Pines

### Pinus pinaster (Maritime Pine / Pino marittimo)

**Suitability: EXCELLENT -- possibly the best Mediterranean candidate for niwaki.**

- Native to Sardinia, dominant in coastal and low-altitude areas
- Long needles (15-25 cm) give dramatic cloud effect
- Naturally develops sculptural form with age (wind-swept trunks, asymmetric canopy)
- Growth habit comparable to Pinus thunbergii (Japanese black pine), which is THE classic niwaki pine in Japan
- Already adapted to poor, sandy, acidic soils

**Technique for pine niwaki (applies to all Mediterranean pines):**

1. **Candle pruning (midoritsumi)** -- In spring (April-May in Sardinia), new growth emerges as "candles." Break or cut candles to 1/3 to 1/2 their length before needles open. This controls extension growth and encourages back-budding.
2. **Needle plucking (momijigari/ha-muki)** -- In autumn (October-November), remove old needles from previous years, leaving only current-year growth. This opens up the branch structure, lets light in, and creates the "clean" look.
3. **Branch selection** -- Remove crossing branches, downward-growing branches, and competing leaders. Maintain the principle of "triangles within triangles" -- each cloud pad should be roughly triangular in profile.
4. **Branch training** -- Use guy wires, weights, or bamboo props to position main branches. Pines are flexible when young. Once set (2-3 years in position), the branch holds its shape.

**Key difference from Japan:** In Sardinia's climate, the growth season is longer. Candle growth may start as early as March and a second flush can occur in September. Monitor growth rather than following a calendar.

### Pinus pinea (Stone Pine / Pino domestico)

**Suitability: GOOD with caveats.**

- The natural umbrella canopy is already "Japanese" in feel -- Pinus pinea naturally creates a flat-topped, cloud-like silhouette
- This is both advantage (less work needed to create the basic form) and limitation (the natural form is so strong that fighting it is futile)
- Best approach: ENHANCE the natural form rather than imposing a multi-pad cloud shape
- Thin the canopy from below, clean the trunk, emphasize the horizontal branching
- Works beautifully as a single-canopy "tama-zukuri" (ball-shaped) or "kabusha-style" (mushroom/umbrella) niwaki

**Technique specifics:**
- Do NOT attempt multi-pad cloud pruning on mature P. pinea -- the branching architecture doesn't support it
- On young trees (< 10 years), you CAN train multiple pads if you start early
- Candle pruning works the same as P. pinaster
- P. pinea's shorter needles (10-15 cm) make it slightly easier to create dense pads

### Pinus halepensis (Aleppo Pine)

**Suitability: MODERATE.**

- Common in eastern Sardinia and coastal areas
- Lighter, more open growth habit -- less dense than P. pinaster
- Can be cloud-pruned but produces a more airy, less sculptural result
- Better suited to a naturalistic windswept (fukinagashi) style than formal cloud pads

### Pruning Season for Mediterranean Pines

| Operation | Japan timing | Sardinia timing | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|-------|
| Candle pruning | May-June | April-May (early) or May-June (normal) | Earlier start due to warmer springs |
| Needle plucking | Oct-Nov | October-November | Similar timing |
| Structural pruning | Dec-Feb | December-February | Dormant season, same principle |
| Second candle flush | Rare | September (possible) | Monitor -- break if unwanted |

### Timeline

- **Year 1-2:** Establish basic branch structure. Remove unwanted branches. Begin candle pruning.
- **Year 3-5:** Cloud pads become recognizable. Annual needle plucking begins. Wire/weight branches into position.
- **Year 5-10:** Mature niwaki form. Maintenance pruning only.
- **From a mature existing tree:** 3-5 years to a convincing niwaki, assuming good bone structure.

## 2. Niwaki on Native Sardinian Trees

### Quercus ilex (Holm Oak / Leccio)

**Suitability: EXCELLENT -- top recommendation for broadleaf niwaki.**

- Evergreen, dense foliage, fine-textured leaves
- Responds brilliantly to pruning -- resprouts vigorously from old wood
- The Japanese equivalent is Quercus glauca and Q. myrsinifolia (Japanese evergreen oaks), which are traditional niwaki species
- Already used in cloud pruning in southern France extensively
- Can be cloud-pruned into dramatic multi-pad forms
- Tolerates heavy shaping and recovers quickly

**Technique:**
- Prune in late spring (May-June) after the first growth flush has hardened
- Can be pruned again in late summer (August-September) if vigorous
- Select 3-7 main branches, strip interior growth, shape terminal foliage into rounded or flat-topped pads
- Q. ilex bark becomes attractively rugged with age -- a feature to expose and celebrate
- Use secateurs (not shears) for a cleaner look -- shears leave visible cut marks on the relatively large leaves

**Advantage over Japanese species:** Q. ilex is drought-tolerant and needs zero irrigation once established. Japanese evergreen oaks need more moisture.

### Quercus suber (Cork Oak / Sughera)

**Suitability: GOOD -- for enhancement rather than formal niwaki.**

- The bark is the star feature -- niwaki pruning should maximize bark visibility
- Natural sculptural form (gnarled, twisted trunk) is already highly aesthetic
- Cloud pruning is possible but the sparse, irregular branching means fewer pads
- Best approach: selective branch removal to expose trunk architecture + light foliage shaping
- Think "wabi-sabi" aesthetic rather than formal geometric clouds
- Semi-evergreen in Sardinia (drops some leaves in severe winters)

**Technique:**
- Prune in late winter (February) -- avoid disturbing cork bark
- NEVER strip cork from a tree you're training as niwaki -- the intact bark is the aesthetic
- Thin canopy from below, expose main limbs, create 2-4 loose cloud pads

### Arbutus unedo (Strawberry Tree / Corbezzolo)

**Suitability: VERY GOOD.**

- Evergreen, compact growth, beautiful reddish bark that peels
- Flowers and fruits simultaneously (white flowers + red fruits in autumn/winter) -- spectacular
- Responds well to pruning -- resprouts from old wood
- Small tree (3-8m) -- perfect scale for a residential Japanese garden
- Multi-stem form is natural and highly suited to niwaki
- The bark resembles that of Stewartia pseudocamellia (a prized Japanese garden tree)

**Technique:**
- Prune after fruiting (February-March) to preserve the autumn/winter display
- Strip lower branches to expose the beautiful bark
- Shape canopy into 2-5 cloud pads
- The multi-stem habit means you can create a "grove" effect (hayashi-zukuri) with 3-5 stems

### Myrtus communis (Myrtle / Mirto)

**Suitability: EXCELLENT for hedges/karikomi; GOOD for individual niwaki.**

- Very small, aromatic, evergreen leaves -- perfect for fine-textured cloud shapes
- Dense growth habit -- can be clipped into precise forms
- Traditional Sardinian hedge plant -- strong cultural resonance
- The direct Mediterranean equivalent of Ilex crenata (Japanese holly) for hedging
- Can be shaped as o-karikomi (large abstract flowing forms) or individual cloud-pruned specimens

**Technique:**
- Clip 2-3 times per year (May, July, September) for hedge/karikomi forms
- For individual niwaki, prune after flowering (August) to get the berries for mirto production
- Slow-growing enough that maintenance is manageable
- Can be trained as a small standard (single trunk + cloud head) or multi-stem

### Ilex aquifolium (Holly / Agrifoglio)

**Suitability: GOOD -- the European substitute for Ilex crenata.**

- Ilex crenata (Japanese holly) is one of the most-used niwaki plants in Japan -- for hedges and individual specimens
- I. aquifolium has much larger, spiny leaves -- a different aesthetic but still workable
- Better suited to cloud pruning as individual specimens than as fine hedging
- Grows in Sardinia's mountain areas (Gennargentu, Limbara) rather than coastal zones
- Slow-growing -- requires patience

**Important note:** For the closest substitute to I. crenata, consider Phillyrea latifolia (see below) rather than I. aquifolium. Phillyrea has small leaves much more similar to I. crenata.

**Alternative -- Phillyrea latifolia / P. angustifolia (Fillirea)**

- Native to Sardinia, evergreen, small olive-like leaves
- The RHS specifically recommends Phillyrea latifolia for cloud pruning
- Dense growth, responds beautifully to clipping
- More drought-tolerant than Ilex crenata
- THIS is the Mediterranean Ilex crenata replacement -- strongly recommended

### Olea europaea (Olive / Olivo)

**Suitability: SPECTACULAR -- the signature Mediterranean niwaki.**

- Ancient, gnarled olive trees trained as niwaki are among the most dramatic garden specimens possible
- The twisted trunk, silver-green foliage, and enormous age of old olives create instant "wabi-sabi"
- Cloud pruning reveals the extraordinary trunk architecture
- Already extensively used in Mediterranean garden design (French Riviera, Puglia, Barcelona)
- Sardinia has many old olive trees (some 500+ years) that could be enhanced

**Technique:**
- Best to start with a mature tree (10+ years) that has interesting trunk character
- In spring (March-April), remove suckers (polloni) from base and trunk
- Select 3-7 main branches, strip all interior growth (fogliame interno)
- Shape terminal foliage into rounded cloud pads using secateurs
- Maintenance: trim 2x per year (spring and late summer)
- Critical: DO NOT over-thin -- olives need enough leaf mass to sustain the tree
- If the tree fruits, decide whether to keep fruiting branches or sacrifice fruit for form

**Timeline:** An old olive with good structure can look like convincing niwaki in 1-2 years of work. A young olive takes 5-10 years.

## 3. Jake Hobson's Work

### The Book

**"Niwaki: Pruning, Training and Shaping Trees the Japanese Way"** by Jake Hobson (Timber Press, 2007, ISBN: 978-0881928358).

This is the definitive English-language reference on niwaki. The RHS specifically recommends it on their cloud pruning advice page (T7 -- RHS).

**Mediterranean content:** The book is primarily focused on Japanese species and techniques as practiced in Japan, with a UK/European adaptation perspective. It does NOT have a dedicated Mediterranean chapter. However:

- Hobson explicitly states that niwaki principles can be applied to garden trees "all over the world" and are not restricted to Japanese gardens
- He covers Pinus (general genus) in detail -- the techniques for P. thunbergii transfer directly to P. pinaster and P. pinea
- He covers broadleaf evergreen pruning that applies to Q. ilex, Phillyrea, and other Mediterranean broadleaves
- The section on Ilex crenata pruning is directly applicable to Phillyrea and Myrtus

### Jake Hobson / Niwaki.com

Jake Hobson is a British garden designer and pruning specialist based in Dorset, UK. He:

- Trained with Japanese gardeners in Japan
- Founded niwaki.com (also a tool company selling Japanese garden tools)
- Has worked extensively on cloud pruning projects in the UK and Europe
- His website jakehobson.com redirects to niwaki.com (confirmed: 302 redirect)
- Niwaki.com was behind Cloudflare protection and could not be scraped, but the company sells:
  - Japanese pruning saws (noko-giri)
  - Secateurs (sentei-basami)
  - Topiary shears (karikomi-basami)
  - Trimming shears (ueki-basami)

### Hobson on Mediterranean adaptation

While Hobson's published work does not focus specifically on Mediterranean species, the principles he teaches are explicitly universal. His approach emphasizes:

1. **Read the tree** -- work with its natural growth habit, not against it
2. **Less is more** -- remove less than you think; you can always take more off next year
3. **Triangulation** -- every branch, every pad, every whole tree should suggest a triangle
4. **Odd numbers** -- 3, 5, or 7 pads, not 2, 4, or 6
5. **Asymmetry** -- perfection is NOT the goal; natural irregularity is

These principles apply identically to Mediterranean species.

## 4. Cloud Pruning (O-karikomi) Technique -- Step by Step

### Terminology Clarification

- **Niwaki** = garden tree; the overall art of shaping garden trees
- **Cloud pruning** = shaping individual pads of foliage to resemble clouds (sometimes called "tama-mono" for ball shapes)
- **O-karikomi** = large-scale clipped forms, usually hedges shaped into abstract flowing/rolling shapes (like "green waves")
- **Karikomi** = smaller-scale clipped forms

Cloud pruning of individual trees and o-karikomi of hedges are related but distinct techniques.

### Step-by-Step: Cloud Pruning a Mature Tree (Beginner)

**Prerequisite:** A tree with visible branch structure and some age (5+ years minimum). An old olive, a mature Quercus ilex, or a well-established pine are ideal starting points.

**Step 1: Observation (1-2 weeks)**
- Study the tree from all angles at different times of day
- Photograph it from 4 sides + above if possible
- Identify the main structural branches (usually 3-7)
- Look for natural "cloud" formations already present
- Decide which branches to keep and which to remove

**Step 2: Structural Pruning (winter/early spring -- one session)**
- Using a pruning saw, remove unwanted main branches
- Cut flush to the trunk or parent branch (no stubs)
- Remove: crossing branches, vertically competing branches, branches growing inward
- Keep: branches that create an open, asymmetric framework
- RULE: never remove more than 1/3 of the total canopy in one session
- Stand back frequently and assess

**Step 3: Interior Cleaning**
- Remove all small branches and twigs growing from the interior of remaining branches
- Strip growth from the lower 2/3 of each main branch, leaving foliage only at the tips
- This exposes the branch architecture -- the "bones" of the tree

**Step 4: Pad Shaping (late spring/early summer)**
- Shape the remaining foliage at branch tips into rounded or slightly flat-topped pads
- Use secateurs for broadleaves, shears for fine-textured plants
- For pines: combine candle pruning (spring) + needle plucking (autumn)
- Each pad should be slightly convex on top, flat or slightly concave underneath
- Vary pad sizes -- lower pads larger, upper pads smaller (natural tree proportions)

**Step 5: Refinement (ongoing, 2-3 years)**
- Trim pads 1-2 times per year to maintain shape
- As pads fill out, they become denser and more cloud-like
- Remove any regrowth from cleaned trunk/branch areas ("watersprouts" / succhioni)
- Gradually refine pad shapes each year

### Common Mistakes

1. **Removing too much at once.** The #1 beginner error. Remove less than you think necessary. You can always take more off next year -- you cannot put it back.
2. **Symmetry obsession.** Western gardeners instinctively try to make things symmetrical. Niwaki should be deliberately asymmetric.
3. **Even numbers of pads.** Use 3, 5, or 7 pads -- never 2, 4, or 6. Odd numbers create visual tension.
4. **Flat-bottomed pads.** The underside of each cloud should be slightly concave and clean, showing the branch below.
5. **Wrong timing.** Pruning pines when candles are NOT active (summer/winter) misses the control point. Pruning broadleaves when actively growing (early spring) causes excessive bleeding.
6. **Neglecting the trunk.** The trunk and main branches are as important as the foliage. Clean them, expose bark, remove lichen if desired.
7. **Using shears on large-leaved species.** Shears cut through leaves, leaving ugly brown half-leaves. Use secateurs for Quercus ilex, Arbutus, Olea.
8. **Impatience.** A convincing niwaki takes 3-5 years minimum. Embrace the process.

### Years of Practice

- A beginner can produce a recognizable (if rough) cloud-pruned specimen after 1-2 sessions with guidance
- Confident, independent cloud pruning: 2-3 years of practice
- Mastery (the ability to "read" any tree and know immediately how to shape it): 5-10+ years
- In Japan, apprentice gardeners train for 10 years before being trusted with important specimens

- **Juniperus oxycedrus** (ginepro rosso) -- similar to J. phoenicea but with needle-like leaves.
- **Pistacia lentiscus** (lentisco) -- small-leaved, excellent for bonsai, very Sardinian.
- **Arbutus unedo** (corbezzolo) -- used in bonsai; beautiful bark and flowers.
- **Myrtus communis** (mirto) -- small leaves, fragrant, good bonsai material.
- **Quercus ilex** (leccio) -- used in bonsai though the leaves are relatively large.
- **Phillyrea latifolia** (fillirea) -- excellent fine-leaved bonsai material.

### Why a Bonsai Contact is Valuable for Niwaki

A Sardinian bonsai practitioner would understand:
- How each native species responds to pruning (which species back-bud reliably, which don't)
- Local growth patterns (when species flush, when they're dormant)
- Where to source native plant material
- The aesthetics of wabi-sabi as applied to Mediterranean forms
- Wire training techniques that transfer directly to niwaki branch positioning

**Recommendation:** Contact UBI Sardegna or attend a local bonsai exhibition to find an experienced practitioner willing to consult on niwaki work.

## 6. Japanese-Style Hedge Pruning (Karikomi)

### What is Karikomi?

Karikomi is the Japanese art of clipping shrubs and hedges into rounded, flowing forms. Unlike Western topiary (geometric: balls, cubes, cones), karikomi creates organic, undulating shapes that suggest natural landscapes -- rolling hills, waves, clouds hovering over the ground.

The most famous examples are at Shishinden Palace (Kyoto) and Raikyuji Temple (Takahashi).

### O-karikomi with Mediterranean Species

**Myrtus communis -- IDEAL for karikomi**
- Fine-textured leaves (similar to Satsuki azalea, the classic karikomi plant in Japan)
- Dense growth allows precise shaping
- Aromatic when clipped (bonus)
- Already used as hedging throughout Sardinia
- Clip 2-3 times per growing season (May, July, September)
- Can create rolling "cloud hedge" forms along paths or boundaries
- The traditional Sardinian mirto hedge, given rounded rather than flat-topped clipping, becomes instant karikomi

**Phillyrea latifolia / P. angustifolia -- EXCELLENT**
- Very small leaves, dense growth
- Specifically recommended by the RHS for cloud pruning (T7 -- RHS)
- More vigorous than myrtle -- clip 2-3 times per year
- Can create large-scale flowing forms (2-3m high)
- Dark, glossy foliage provides excellent contrast with stone or gravel

**Pittosporum tobira -- GOOD**
- Not native to Sardinia but widely naturalized
- Leathery leaves, dense growth
- Excellent for larger karikomi forms
- Fragrant flowers (bonus)
- Clip after flowering (June) + maintenance clip (September)

**Buxus sempervirens (Bosso) -- CLASSIC**
- The classic European cloud/topiary plant
- Fine-textured, very dense, slow-growing
- RHS recommends for cloud pruning (T7 -- RHS)
- Caveat: box tree caterpillar (Cydalima perspectalis) has devastated box across Mediterranean Europe. Check current status in Sardinia before investing in box.
- If box is viable, it's the easiest species to cloud-prune

**Laurus nobilis (Alloro) -- MODERATE**
- Dense evergreen, responds to pruning
- Larger leaves make it less suited to fine karikomi
- Better for larger-scale forms (2m+ mounds)

### Creating the "Cloud Hedge" Effect

**Starting from an existing flat-topped hedge:**
1. Mark out the desired undulating profile with string or spray paint
2. Cut the top to follow the wave pattern -- alternating high and low points
3. Round over the edges of each "hill" with shears
4. Over 2-3 years, the rounded forms fill out and become organic
5. Gradually increase the contrast between peaks and valleys each year

**Starting from scratch:**
1. Plant at 30-50 cm spacing (depending on species)
2. Allow to grow to desired height (1-2 m typically)
3. Begin shaping into undulating forms from year 2-3
4. The "cloud hedge" effect typically takes 3-5 years to look convincing

### Comparison with Sardinian Hedge Traditions

Traditional Sardinian hedging uses:
- Mirto (myrtle) -- flat-topped or informal
- Lentisco (mastic) -- informal boundary
- Rosmarino (rosemary) -- low informal hedge
- Alloro (bay) -- formal tall hedge

The transition from flat-topped to karikomi-style is purely a matter of clipping technique -- no new plants needed. A mirto hedge that has been flat-clipped for years can be gradually reshaped into flowing cloud forms over 2-3 seasons.

## 7. The Monaco Japanese Garden -- Lessons for Sardinia

### Key Facts (T7 -- Wikipedia)

- Designed by **Yasuo Beppu** (winner, Flower Exhibition of Osaka 1990)
- Commissioned by **Prince Rainier III** in 1994
- Located on Avenue Princesse Grace, Larvotto, Monaco
- 0.7 hectares (1.7 acres)
- Features: stylised mountain, hill, waterfall, beach, brook, Zen garden, tea house
- Climate: Mediterranean (nearly identical to northeastern Sardinia)

### Lesson: Mediterranean Plants in Japanese Forms

The Monaco garden is proof of concept that Japanese garden aesthetics work with Mediterranean climate and (partially) Mediterranean plant palette. The garden uses:
- Mediterranean pines trained in Japanese forms
- Local stone arranged in traditional Japanese placement
- Water features adapted to Mediterranean rainfall patterns (less water than Japanese originals)
- Integration of Mediterranean light quality (brighter, harsher than Japan) into the design

### Direct Applications to a Sardinian Project

1. **Climate match:** Monaco (Koppen Csa) is climatically very close to coastal Sardinia (also Csa). What works in Monaco works in Sardinia.
2. **Stone:** Sardinian granite is excellent for Japanese-style stone placement (ishi-gumi). The grey-pink tones of Gallura granite are reminiscent of Japanese granite.
3. **Water:** Sardinia has less water than Japan. Design for dry-stream beds (kare-sansui / dry landscape) rather than water features. Alternatively, use recirculating systems.
4. **Light:** Mediterranean light is brighter and harder than Japan's filtered light. Use overhead pine canopy to create dappled shade (komorebi) -- exactly what niwaki pines provide.

## 8. Recommended Action Plan

### Phase 1: Quick Wins (Year 1)

1. **Select 1-2 existing mature olives** with interesting trunk structure. Begin cloud pruning immediately (March-April). These will be the garden's signature specimens.
2. **Convert existing mirto hedges** (if present) from flat-top to karikomi undulating forms. Begin with gentle reshaping.
3. **Plant 3-5 Phillyrea latifolia** as future cloud-pruned specimens or karikomi mounds. 1.5-2m specimens if budget allows.

### Phase 2: Structure (Years 1-3)

4. **Select 1-2 Pinus pinaster** (ideally existing semi-mature trees). Begin niwaki training: first structural pruning in winter, first candle pruning in spring.
5. **Select 1 Arbutus unedo** (multi-stem if possible). Begin bark exposure and cloud pad formation.
6. **Plant Quercus ilex** in key structural positions. Buy the largest specimens budget allows -- Q. ilex grows slowly but responds to pruning immediately.

### Phase 3: Refinement (Years 3-5)

7. Annual maintenance of all specimens
8. Add secondary plantings: ground cover (Thymus, Rosmarinus prostrate), gravel areas, stone placement
9. By Year 5, the garden should have recognizable Japanese character

### Phase 4: Contact a Bonsai Practitioner

- Attend a UBI Sardegna event or contact Club Bonsai Cagliari
- Find someone experienced with native species pruning
- Offer a consulting day to assess your trees and advise on niwaki technique

## Serendipitous Connections

**Niwaki and fractal geometry.** Cloud-pruned trees exhibit self-similar branching patterns at multiple scales -- each pad is a miniature tree, each branch a miniature trunk. This connects to Benoit Mandelbrot's observation that trees are natural fractals. The niwaki gardener is, in effect, editing a fractal structure to emphasize its self-similarity. A mathematical treatment of optimal branching angles in pruned trees (minimizing stress, maximizing light capture) would be an interesting applied geometry problem.

**Olive niwaki and Sardinian cultural identity.** The olive tree is central to Sardinian identity (olivastro millenario di Luras, estimated 3000-4000 years old). Training olives as niwaki is a fusion of two deep cultural traditions of tree reverence -- Japanese (sacred trees marked with shimenawa ropes) and Sardinian/Mediterranean (sacred groves, the olive as symbol of peace and endurance). This is not mere appropriation but genuine cultural synthesis.

**Bonsai, niwaki, and information compression.** Both bonsai and niwaki are forms of "lossy compression" of a tree's information content -- removing branches to preserve the essential structure while discarding redundancy. This is analogous to dimensionality reduction techniques (PCA, UMAP) in data science: you lose detail but gain legibility. The master gardener's skill is in knowing which branches are "signal" and which are "noise" -- exactly the problem in feature selection for ML models.
