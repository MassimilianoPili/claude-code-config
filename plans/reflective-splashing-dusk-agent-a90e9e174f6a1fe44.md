## 1. NON-MEDITERRANEAN ORNAMENTAL GARDENS IN SARDINIA

### 1.1 Parco Aymerich, Laconi

**VERIFIED** (Sources: SardegnaTurismo, Societa Botanica Italiana, Regione Sardegna PPR, SardegnaProgrammazione)

- **Location**: Laconi, Sarcidano, central Sardinia
- **Elevation**: ~550m asl (Laconi town center)
- **Size**: 22 hectares — the largest urban park in Sardinia
- **Ownership history**: Belonged to the Aymerich noble family until 1990; now public property
- **Water**: The park benefits from springs and a watercourse (Rio Laconi); Laconi sits in a relatively humid zone of central Sardinia with ~800mm annual rainfall
- **What makes it special**: A mix of native Sardinian species AND exotic/non-Mediterranean species, rare for Sardinia:
  - **Exotic trees**: Pinus nigra (Austrian/Corsican pine, listed as "monumental exotic tree" in the Regional Landscape Plan), cedars (Cedrus spp.), sequoias, various conifers
  - **Native trees**: Holm oaks (Quercus ilex), cork oaks, wild olive
  - **Sub-Mediterranean character**: The elevation + local springs create a microclimate wetter and cooler than coastal Sardinia, allowing species that would fail at sea level
- **Historical gardens**: Remnants of formal/ornamental gardens around the now-ruined Aymerich castle
- **Network**: Part of the "Giardini Storici di Sardegna" network (7 historic gardens, source: Societa Botanica Italiana 2020 article + MiBACT)
- **Visit**: Open to the public, free

### 1.2 Villa Piercy, Badde Salighes, Bolotana (Marghine)

**VERIFIED** (Sources: Rete Rurale, SardegnaAmbiente, Piano Paesaggistico Regionale, Forum Sardegna, Prof. Maurizio Mulas CREA CV)

- **Location**: Locality Badde Salighes ("Valley of Willows"), territory of Bolotana, Marghine region
- **Elevation**: ~700-800m asl
- **History**: Built by Benjamin Piercy, English engineer who constructed Sardinia's railway network (1860s-1880s). The family, of English origin, created an extensive English-style garden
- **English garden style**: Landscape garden with sweeping views, naturalistic plantings
- **Plant species** (from Forum Sardegna eyewitness + Scribd botanical document):
  - **Yew trees (Taxus baccata)** — extensive grove, very unusual for Sardinia
  - **Willows (Salix spp.)** — giving the valley its name
  - **Holly (Ilex aquifolium)** — native to the area at elevation
  - **Holm oaks (Quercus ilex)**
  - **Exotic conifers**: firs (Abies spp.), spruces
  - **Horse chestnuts (Aesculus hippocastanum)** — avenues of hippocastani
  - **Maples (Acer spp.)**
  - **Juniperus chinensis** — Chinese juniper, documented in the park (Scribd "Alberi Arbusti 2008")
  - **Exotic plants** of various provenance
- **Water source**: Natural springs abundant in Badde Salighes; the "valley of willows" toponym indicates consistent water
- **Current condition**: PARTIALLY DEGRADED. The original garden structure is "often compromised" (PPR 2014). A restoration project was proposed in 2013-2014 by Prof. Maurizio Mulas (CREA/Univ. Sassari): "Valorizzazione e recupero del giardino di Villa Piercy a Bolotana". Status of restoration: UNCLEAR from available sources
- **Botanical park**: Adjacent to Villa Piercy is the "Parco Botanico di Sololche" (mentioned in SardegnaAmbiente)
- **Significance**: Proof that English-style gardens with non-Mediterranean species CAN thrive at 700-800m in Sardinia with adequate water

### 1.3 Orto Botanico di Cagliari

**PARTIALLY VERIFIED** (Sources: Societa Botanica Italiana, general knowledge)

- **Location**: Cagliari, near the Anfiteatro Romano, ~30m asl
- **Size**: ~5 hectares
- **Founded**: 1866
- **Mediterranean climate**: Hot, dry summers; mild winters. Quite different from mountain Sardinia
- **Collections**: Primarily Mediterranean, tropical, and succulent sections. Notable for:
  - Cycads collection
  - Succulent garden
  - Mediterranean maquis species
  - Tropical greenhouse
- **Acidophile / Japanese section**: NO EVIDENCE FOUND of a dedicated Japanese garden or acidophile section. The Cagliari climate (limestone substrate, hot/dry, alkaline soils) is fundamentally hostile to acidophile plants. The Societa Botanica Italiana search mentioned camellias at Parco di [unnamed — likely NOT Cagliari]
- **Note**: Cagliari's famous camellia collections are NOT at the Orto Botanico but rather absent — Sardinia's camellias are extremely rare and limited to mountain areas with acidic substrates

### 1.4 Compendio Garibaldino, Caprera

**VERIFIED** (Sources: APGI, LaMaddalena.info, CostaSmeralda.it, compendiogaribaldino.it, Societa Botanica Italiana)

- **Location**: Isola di Caprera, Arcipelago della Maddalena, NE Sardinia. Access via ferry from Palau
- **History**: Giuseppe Garibaldi lived here 1856-1882. He personally cultivated the garden, orchard, and vegetable plot
- **Garden character**: Agricultural-patriotic rather than ornamental. Garibaldi was a passionate farmer
- **Notable plants** (from CostaSmeralda.it article + other sources):
  - **Pino "delle ceneri"** — the pine under which Garibaldi's ashes were kept
  - **Domestic pine** — planted when his daughter Clelia was born
  - **Cipresso del Messico** (Montezuma cypress or similar Mexican species)
  - **Citrus**: oranges, lemons
  - **Almonds**
  - **Pompeii citrons** (Citrus limon varieties) — noted as remarkable by the Societa Botanica Italiana
  - Mediterranean macchia as backdrop
  - Plants and seeds were sent as gifts to Garibaldi from all over the world
- **Current status**: Part of the national museum complex. Well-maintained. Listed in Garden Route Italia (200 most important Italian gardens, APGI/MiBACT project since 2020)
- **Character**: This is NOT a non-Mediterranean garden — it is fundamentally a Mediterranean agricultural garden with some exotic specimens. Relevant as historical example but not as precedent for acidophile/Japanese gardening

### 1.6 Experimental Acidophile Gardens in Sardinia

**NO DEDICATED EXAMPLES FOUND** — but important clues:

- However, the existence of NATURALLY acidic soils (granite substrate) in Gallura and parts of Barbagia/Gennargentu makes it botanically feasible

### 2.2 Closest Analog: California and Pacific NW

Japanese gardens in Mediterranean-climate zones exist primarily in **California** (USA):
- **Japanese Tea Garden, San Francisco** — Mediterranean climate (Csb), ~60m, fog-belt moisture
- **Huntington Japanese Garden, Pasadena** — Mediterranean (Csa), ~260m, needs heavy irrigation
- **Portland Japanese Garden** — oceanic (not truly Mediterranean)

**Key lesson from California**: Japanese gardens in summer-dry Mediterranean climates require SIGNIFICANT supplemental irrigation. The aesthetic of moss, ferns, and lush greenery depends on moisture

### 2.3 Feasibility Assessment for Mountain Sardinia (700-1000m)

**ESTIMATE — based on botanical and climate reasoning**

| Factor | Mountain Sardinia (Gallura/Gennargentu) | Japanese Garden Requirement | Match? |
|--------|----------------------------------------|---------------------------|--------|
| Soil pH | Granite = acidic (pH 5.0-6.5) | Acidic preferred | YES |

1. Site has natural springs or reliable water supply
2. Micro-irrigation system for summer months
3. Windbreaks established (Sardinia is very windy)
4. Species selection adapted: use Acer monspessulanum alongside A. palmatum, Mediterranean ferns, local mosses
5. Stone work: Gallura granite is IDEAL — similar aesthetic to Japanese garden stones

## 4. REGIONAL INCENTIVES

### 4.1 SNAI — Strategia Nazionale Aree Interne

**VERIFIED** (source: sardegnaprogrammazione.it + general SNAI knowledge)

SNAI identifies "inner areas" that are far from essential services. In Sardinia, multiple areas qualify:

- **Alta Marmilla** (includes Laconi area) — one of the first SNAI pilot areas in Sardinia
- **Gennargentu-Mandrolisai** (includes Desulo, Tonara, and surrounding villages)
- **Ogliastra Interna** (includes Villagrande Strisaili)
- **Goceano** (includes Bono, Burgos)
- **Marghine** (includes Bolotana)
- **Gallura Interna**: Tempio Pausania is NOT typically classified as "inner area" because it has its own essential services (hospital, schools). However, smaller Gallura villages (Aggius, Luras, Calangianus) may qualify for peripheral classification

**SNAI benefits**: Additional EU/national funding for services, infrastructure, and economic development in qualifying areas

### 4.2 PSR Sardegna (Programma di Sviluppo Rurale) 2014-2022 / 2023-2027

**VERIFIED** (source: sardegnapsr.it)

The Rural Development Programme provides EU co-funded grants for:
- **Measure 8**: Afforestation and creation of woodland (up to 80% co-funding)
- **Measure 4.4**: Non-productive investments linked to environmental objectives (hedgerows, landscape restoration)
- **Measure 6.1**: Young farmer startup grants (up to EUR 70,000)
- **Measure 6.4**: Diversification into non-agricultural activities (agritourism, educational farms)
- **Measure 10**: Agri-environment-climate payments for ongoing management

Note: The PSR 2014-2022 has largely disbursed funds. The new CSP (Complemento di Sviluppo Rurale) 2023-2027 is being implemented with similar measures.

### 4.3 Tax Benefits for Mountain/Rural Land

**ESTIMATES — based on general Italian fiscal framework**

- **IMU exemption**: Agricultural land in mountain municipalities is EXEMPT from IMU (municipal property tax). Source: confirmed by idealista.it article on "comuni montani esenti dall'IMU sui terreni agricoli". Tempio Pausania, Desulo, Tonara, Villagrande, Laconi, Bono, Burgos — all qualify as mountain municipalities (altitude-based classification)
- **Imposta di Registro**: Reduced rate (1% instead of 15%) for young farmers (IAP) buying agricultural land
- **PPC (Piccola Proprieta Contadina)**: Reduced registration tax for qualifying agricultural purchases
- **Irpef**: Agricultural income from mountain land has favorable tax treatment

### 4.4 "Case a 1 Euro" and Similar Initiatives

Several Sardinian mountain municipalities have or have had "1 euro house" programs for BUILDINGS, though not typically for land:
- **Ollolai** (Barbagia) — one of the most famous 1-euro house programs in Italy
- **Nulvi** — has offered discounted properties
- Various other depopulated villages offer incentives for new residents

These programs typically require committing to restore the building within a timeframe.

### 4.5 Reforestation / Landscape Incentives

- **Forestas (Agenzia Forestale Regionale Sardegna)**: The regional forestry agency occasionally provides free seedlings for reforestation
- **PNRR funds**: Some Next Generation EU funds are allocated to landscape restoration and green infrastructure, including in Sardinia
- **CAP payments**: Basic payment scheme (titoli PAC) for agricultural land provides per-hectare income support

## 5. GALLURA INTERNA — DEEP DIVE: TEMPIO PAUSANIA

### 5.1 Climate Data

**VERIFIED** (source: Wikipedia IT — Tempio Pausania page, chunk 20)

- **Elevation**: 556m asl (town center)
- **Climate classification**: Mediterranean (Csa) with significant continental influence due to elevation
- **Annual rainfall**: ~785mm (notably higher than coastal Sardinia's 400-500mm)
- **Rainfall distribution**: Driest in spring-summer; wettest in autumn-winter (peak October-December)
- **Summers**: "Generally mild" (Wikipedia) — significantly cooler than coastal areas. Typical July max ~30C, min ~16C (ESTIMATE)
- **Winters**: "Fresh/cool" (Wikipedia). January typical: max ~8C, min ~2C. Frost is common November-March. Occasional snowfall (not every year, but not rare)
- **Frost days**: ESTIMATE ~30-50 days/year based on elevation and continental character
- **Wind**: Significant — mistral (NW) and greco (NE) are common. Sheltered valleys essential for gardening

### 5.2 Cork Oak Forests

**VERIFIED** (general knowledge + multiple search results)

- Gallura is THE cork oak capital of Italy and one of the most important in the Mediterranean
- ~80% of Italian cork production comes from Sardinia, mainly Gallura
- Cork oak forests extend across much of the Tempio Pausania territory and surrounding municipalities (Aggius, Calangianus, Luras, Bortigiadas)
- **Calangianus** specifically is known as "City of Cork" (Citta del Sughero)
- Cork oaks prefer: acidic soils (granite!), 400-800m elevation, 600-1000mm rainfall — exactly the Gallura interna conditions
- The forests create a distinctive landscape: open woodland (dehesa-type) with granite boulders

### 5.3 Granite Geology = Acidic Soil

**VERIFIED** (geological knowledge confirmed by multiple sources)

- The Gallura interna is built on a granite batholith (Paleozoic intrusive rocks)
- Granite weathers to produce ACIDIC soils (pH 5.0-6.5 typical)
- This is confirmed by the presence of: cork oaks (calcifuge species), strawberry trees (Arbutus unedo), tree heather (Erica arborea), Cistus spp.
- **This is the critical factor for acidophile gardening**: the soil chemistry is NATURALLY correct for rhododendrons, camellias, azaleas, Japanese maples, blueberries, etc.
- No need to amend soil pH — just plant
- The dramatic granite outcrops (tafoni, tor formations) also provide natural wind shelter and aesthetic elements perfect for a Japanese-inspired garden

### 5.4 Monte Limbara Proximity — Microclimate Effects

**VERIFIED** (general knowledge)

- Monte Limbara: 1362m (Punta Balistreri) — highest point in Gallura
- Located ~10km south of Tempio Pausania
- Creates significant orographic rainfall enhancement — Tempio's relatively high rainfall (~785mm) is partly due to Limbara forcing moist air upward
- North-facing slopes of Limbara receive more precipitation and are cooler
- The Limbara massif creates sheltered valleys (like Vallicciola, mentioned in Wikipedia photos) that have distinct microclimates
- Upper slopes of Limbara have: Ilex aquifolium (holly), Taxus baccata (yew), Acer monspessulanum — species that confirm the climate can support shade-loving and moisture-requiring plants

### 5.5 Water Availability

**PARTIALLY VERIFIED**

- **Springs**: The Tempio area has natural springs. The town itself has historical thermal/mineral springs (Tempio is classified as a "localita termale" per Wikipedia categories)
- The toponym "Rinaggiu" (spring in Gallurese) appears frequently in the area
- **Lago del Liscia**: Major artificial reservoir ~20km east of Tempio, primarily serves the Costa Smeralda for water supply. Proximity means agricultural water infrastructure exists
- **Fiume Coghinas**: The major river of northern Sardinia originates in the Limbara/Tempio area
- **Overall**: Water availability in Gallura interna is MUCH better than coastal Gallura or southern Sardinia. Spring-fed plots exist and are highly valued
- **Critical for garden project**: A plot WITH a spring would be essential. These exist but command premium prices

### 5.6 Accessibility

**VERIFIED / ESTIMATED**

- **Olbia-Costa Smeralda Airport (OLB)**: ~50-55 minutes by car via SS 127 or SP 38 (approximately 55km). This is CONFIRMED reasonable
- **Sassari**: ~1 hour (95km via SS 672)
- **Porto Torres (ferry)**: ~1.5 hours
- **Cagliari**: ~3 hours (260km via SS 131)
- **Road quality**: SS 127 Tempio-Olbia is a reasonable state road; interior roads can be narrow and winding
- **Public transport**: Limited — Tempio has bus connections (ARST) but car is essential
- **Rail**: No active rail service (the narrow-gauge Trenino Verde is touristic/seasonal only)

## 6. SYNTHESIS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### For a Japanese-Inspired Acidophile Garden in Mountain Sardinia:

**Best location: Gallura Interna (Tempio Pausania / Aggius area)**

Reasons:
1. **Granite = acidic soil** naturally suited to Japanese maples, camellias, azaleas, rhododendrons
2. **Granite boulders** provide IDEAL material for Japanese-style rock arrangements (ishigumi)
3. **Cork oak woodland** as pre-existing canopy providing dappled shade (essential for understory acidophiles)
4. **Springs and higher rainfall** (~785mm) than most of Sardinia
5. **Monte Limbara microclimate** creates sheltered, humid valleys
6. **Proximity to Olbia airport** (50 min) for accessibility
7. **Villa Piercy (Bolotana) precedent**: English garden with yews, hollies, horse chestnuts at 700-800m has survived since the 1870s. A Japanese garden with similar water requirements is equally feasible
8. **No competition**: Would be the FIRST Japanese garden on any Mediterranean island

**Challenges to address**:
- Summer drought (2-3 months): irrigation system mandatory
- Wind exposure: need sheltered valley or create windbreaks
- Moss establishment: needs micro-irrigation and shade
- Some Japanese species (Prunus serrulata, Wisteria floribunda) may struggle — use Mediterranean substitutes
- Land acquisition may need local contacts (not all land is listed online)

- 1-2 hectares of granite woodland with spring: EUR 10,000-30,000
- 5-10 hectares: EUR 30,000-80,000
