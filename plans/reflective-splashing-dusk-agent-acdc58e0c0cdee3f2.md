# Research: Highland Sardinia — Climate, Vegetation, and Gardening Feasibility

## Executive Summary

Highland Sardinia (800-1000m in Barbagia/Gennargentu) has a **warm-summer Mediterranean climate (Koppen Csb)** — cooler and wetter than the coast, but still fundamentally Mediterranean in its rainfall regime. The critical constraint for lush green gardens is **severe summer drought** (July receives only ~10mm of rain), not temperature or altitude. Without irrigation, achieving Alpine-style saturated green lawns and gardens is not feasible. With irrigation, significant greenery is possible, but the natural palette remains Mediterranean/sub-Mediterranean rather than temperate-continental.

**Epistemic status:** Well-established climate data; botanical zonation from peer-reviewed phytosociological literature.
**Confidence:** High for climate data (multiple independent sources converge); Medium for gardening feasibility (synthesized from botanical literature, not direct horticultural trials at this altitude).

The difference is stark: Alpine locations receive 8-12x more rain in July than Fonni.

## 2. Cala Golorize / Baunei — Climate Data

**Altitude:**
- Baunei town center: ~480m a.s.l.
- Cala Golorize beach: sea level (0m)
- The hiking trail descends ~470-500m from the plateau to the beach (multiple hiking guides confirm this)

**Climate:** Typical Mediterranean **Csa** (hot-summer Mediterranean)
- Baunei is warmer than Fonni due to lower altitude and eastern coastal exposure
- Summers: warm, humid, dry, mostly clear
- Winters: long, cool, windy, partly cloudy
- Temperature range over the year: roughly 5-30C (T7 — weatherspark.com snippet)
- At the beach level, summer temperatures reach high 70s-low 80s F (25-31C)
- Rainfall concentrated October-March; poor rainfall overall; Mediterranean regime

**The coastal cliffs** around Cala Golorize have typical Mediterranean vegetation: maquis shrubland, holm oak, juniper, wild olive, mastic, and scattered maritime pine.

## 3. Supramonte Region — Altitude Range and Vegetation

**Altitude range:** The Supramonte is a limestone (karst) mountain range in central-eastern Sardinia, spanning from approximately **100m (coastal gorges/canyons) to ~1463m** (Monte Corrasi near Oliena). The plateau areas typically sit at **800-1200m**.

**Geology:** Mesozoic limestone — this is crucial for vegetation. The karst terrain means:
- Very porous substrate, rapid drainage
- Water disappears underground quickly
- Thin, rocky soils over much of the area
- Spectacular gorges (Gorropu canyon, Su Gorroppu — Europe's deepest canyon)

**Vegetation zones** (synthesized from multiple academic sources, T1-T2 level):

The Supramonte exhibits a clear altitudinal zonation:

### a) Thermomediterranean belt (0-300m, coastal)
- Wild olive (*Olea europaea* var. *sylvestris*), lentisk (*Pistacia lentiscus*), carob
- Low maquis and garrigue
- Euphorbia scrub on cliffs

### b) Mesomediterranean belt (300-800m)
- **Holm oak** (*Quercus ilex*) forest — the dominant formation
- Cork oak (*Quercus suber*) on siliceous substrates (not on limestone)
- Dense maquis: strawberry tree (*Arbutus unedo*), tree heather (*Erica arborea*), *Phillyrea*
- Juniper (*Juniperus oxycedrus*, *J. phoenicea*) on rocky outcrops

### c) Supramediterranean belt (800-1200m)
- **Montane holm oak forest** — still *Quercus ilex* dominated, but with important additions:
  - **Yew** (*Taxus baccata*) — relict populations, EU Priority Habitat 9580* (T1 — Bacchetta 2012, Phytocoenologia; Calvia et al. 2024, Plant Biosystems)
  - **Holly** (*Ilex aquifolium*) — sub-canopy
  - **Downy oak** (*Quercus pubescens*) — deciduous, in some areas
  - *Juniperus oxycedrus* in the understory
- The famous **Campu Su Disterru** (Supramonte di Orgosolo) hosts montane holm-oak forest with these relict species (T1 — Zedda & Sipman, lichens study)
- At Gennargentu, yew trees with trunk diameter >1m have been found (Calvia et al. 2024)

### d) Oromediterranean belt (>1200m, only on highest peaks)
- Low thorny cushion shrubs (*Astragalus*, *Genista*, *Berberis*)
- Mountain grasslands
- Endemic species (Sardinia has ~10% endemic flora)
- No tree line in the Alpine sense — the upper limit is shaped by wind, grazing, and fire rather than cold

**Critical fact:** Even at 1000-1200m in the Supramonte, the dominant tree is STILL holm oak (*Quercus ilex*) — an evergreen sclerophyll adapted to summer drought. This tells you the climate is fundamentally Mediterranean even at altitude.

## 4. Gardening Potential at 800-1000m in Barbagia

### What thrives naturally

At 800-1000m in Barbagia, the natural vegetation is **supramediterranean** — a transitional zone that is cooler and wetter than the coast but still has the Mediterranean summer drought signature. Plants that thrive naturally:

**Trees:**
- Holm oak (*Quercus ilex*) — evergreen, drought-adapted
- Downy oak (*Quercus pubescens*) — deciduous, more in sheltered valleys
- Yew (*Taxus baccata*) — in sheltered, north-facing gorges with seeping moisture
- Holly (*Ilex aquifolium*) — shade-tolerant understory
- Wild cherry (*Prunus avium*) — scattered
- Chestnut (*Castanea sativa*) — historically planted, does well on deeper soils

**Shrubs and herbs:**
- Strawberry tree (*Arbutus unedo*)
- Tree heather (*Erica arborea*)
- *Crataegus monogyna* (hawthorn)
- *Rosa canina* (dog rose)
- *Clematis* species
- Peonies (*Paeonia* — endemic species in Sardinia)
- Various orchids (spring)

**Fruit trees that do well:**
- Walnuts, hazelnuts, chestnuts
- Apples, pears, plums (the "fruits of the mountain" — traditional Barbagia agriculture)
- Cherries
- NOT citrus (too cold in winter)

### Can you achieve Alpine-style lush green?

**Short answer: No, not without significant irrigation. And even with irrigation, the character will be different.**

Here is why:

**1. Summer drought is the binding constraint, not temperature or altitude**

The ~10mm of rain in July, combined with high solar radiation, high evapotranspiration, and often thin/rocky soils, means that any non-adapted vegetation will desiccate rapidly. An English-style lawn requires approximately 25-30mm of water per week in summer. In July, Fonni receives 10mm for the entire month.

**2. Soil characteristics work against you**

Much of highland Sardinia has:
- Thin soils over granite or limestone bedrock
- Poor water retention capacity
- Rocky substrates
- On granite (common around Fonni): acidic, sandy, well-drained soils
- On limestone (Supramonte): karst = instant drainage

**3. The light regime is Mediterranean**

Even at 1000m, Sardinia receives ~424 hours of sunshine in July (nomadseason.com data for Fonni). This is intense, harsh sunlight that desiccates foliage. Alpine locations at the same altitude have more cloud cover and shorter, less intense summer sun.

**4. What irrigation COULD achieve**

With a reliable water source and irrigation system (drip irrigation preferred for efficiency), you could grow:

- **Green lawn:** Possible but high-maintenance. Cool-season grasses (fescues, ryegrass) would survive the winter better than warm-season grasses, but would need heavy irrigation June-September. A **tall fescue** lawn is probably the most realistic option — it is deep-rooted and somewhat drought-tolerant once established.

- **Ornamental trees:** Many temperate deciduous trees can grow here WITH irrigation: maples (*Acer*), birches (*Betula*), even beeches (*Fagus*) in sheltered positions. The chestnut (*Castanea sativa*) is the traditional "green" tree of the Barbagia highlands.

- **Perennial borders:** Hydrangeas, roses, lavender (native Mediterranean), rosemary, salvias. Many Mediterranean aromatic herbs thrive without irrigation.

- **Vegetable gardens (orti):** Traditional in the Barbagia. With irrigation, excellent productivity: tomatoes, peppers, beans, potatoes, zucchini. The cool nights at altitude actually help with flavor and reduce pest pressure.

**5. The resulting aesthetic**

Even with irrigation, the garden will NOT look like:
- A Swiss Alpine meadow (too dry, too much sun, wrong grasses)
- An English cottage garden (ditto, plus wrong soil)
- A Japanese moss garden (far too dry)

It COULD look like:
- A well-maintained **Tuscan hill garden** (similar altitude, similar issues, similar solutions)
- A **Provencal garden** with Mediterranean aromatics + irrigated ornamental areas
- A **chestnut grove** understory with shade-loving plants — this is probably the most natural and sustainable "green" option
- A Mediterranean version of a woodland garden under oaks/chestnuts, with ferns, cyclamen, hellebores, and shade-tolerant groundcovers in the understory

## 5. Key Limiting Factors Summary

| Factor | Severity | Mitigable? |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| **Summer drought (Jul-Aug)** | CRITICAL | Yes, with irrigation — but water availability may be limited |
| **Thin/rocky soils** | HIGH | Partially — raised beds, soil amendment, organic matter addition |
| **Intense summer solar radiation** | MODERATE | Shade structures, tree canopy, north-facing slopes |
| **Winter frost (-3C avg, -18C extreme)** | MODERATE | Limits subtropical plants; not a problem for temperate species |
| **Wind exposure** | MODERATE | Windbreaks (hedges, walls), sheltered microclimates |
| **Fire risk** | MODERATE | Irrigated garden is actually a firebreak; keep defensible space |
| **Water availability** | VARIABLE | Springs and wells exist in the area; municipal water; rainwater harvesting from Oct-May |

## 6. Examples of Green/Lush Forests and Gardens in Highland Sardinia

### Natural lush areas

1. **Foresta di Montes** (Orgosolo, Supramonte, ~800-1200m) — one of the largest holm oak forests in Europe. Dense, green canopy year-round because holm oak is evergreen. The understory can be quite lush in spring and autumn. However, it is NOT the bright-green of a deciduous northern forest — it has the dark, matte green of Mediterranean evergreen forest.

2. **Sas Baddes / Campu Su Disterru** (Supramonte di Orgosolo, ~900-1100m) — montane holm oak with yew and holly. The yew groves (EU Habitat 9580*) are genuinely lush and dark green, reminiscent of northern forest understory. These are relict populations that survive in sheltered gorges where moisture persists year-round from seeping groundwater.

3. **Foresta di Montarbu** (Seui, ~800-1000m) — mixed holm oak and deciduous forest. Green and relatively lush.

4. **Chestnut groves of Aritzo and Desulo** (~700-900m) — these deciduous forests are the closest thing to northern-European "green" landscapes in Sardinia. In spring and summer, the chestnut canopy is a bright, saturated green. Understory with ferns and mosses. However, they lose leaves in autumn/winter.

5. **Bruncu Spina area** (above Fonni, ~1200-1800m) — grasslands and low scrub, NOT lush forest. The highest areas are windswept and sparse.

### Cultivated green areas

There are no famous ornamental gardens in highland Sardinia comparable to, say, the gardens of the Italian Lakes. The horticultural tradition in Barbagia is pastoral/agricultural, not ornamental. However:

- **Kitchen gardens (orti) in Fonni, Desulo, Aritzo** — traditionally irrigated from springs, these can be remarkably productive and green in summer. Vegetables, fruit trees, grape vines along walls.
- **Church and sanctuary gardens** — some maintained green spaces around churches (e.g., Sanctuary of Madonna dei Martiri in Fonni)
- **Giardino Ferraro** (Cagliari, not highland — but the only notable Sardinian botanic garden)

### The honest assessment

No location in highland Sardinia achieves the year-round, saturated green of an Alpine valley or a Northern European garden without irrigation. The natural "green" of Barbagia is:
- **Dark evergreen** from holm oak (year-round but matte/dark)
- **Bright deciduous green** from chestnuts and oaks (April-October only)
- **Brown/golden** in summer grasslands (natural state without irrigation)
