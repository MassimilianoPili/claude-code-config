# Japanese Garden in Sardinian Mountains -- Design Research Report

**Site conditions**: 500-700m altitude, Sardinia (likely Montiferru/Marghine area). Mediterranean mountain climate. 960mm annual rainfall concentrated October-April, dry summers (June-September near-zero rain). Volcanic soil pH 5.5-6.5. Frost to -6C (USDA Zone 8b-9a). Reference ET0 in July: ~5-7 mm/day (T1 -- Montaldo & Oren, Earth's Future 2018, measured 3.4mm/day mean June-September for Sardinian basins, but open-water evaporation is higher).

**Epistemic status**: Practical horticultural guidance synthesized from nursery expertise (T5-T7), Mediterranean Garden Society publications (T5), and peer-reviewed climate/ecology data where available (T1-T2). Plant hardiness data from RHS, USDA, and specialist nurseries.

## 1. PLANT PALETTE

### 1.1 Trees

#### Japanese Maples (Acer palmatum) -- WITH CAVEATS

Japanese maples are NOT naturally drought-tolerant. In your climate they will need summer irrigation and afternoon shade. The volcanic acidic soil (pH 5.5-6.5) is actually ideal for them. The key challenge is the dry summer heat, not winter cold (-6C is within tolerance for most cultivars, Zone 5-8).

**CRITICAL**: Avoid ALL dissectum (laceleaf) varieties -- they scorch badly in dry heat. Stick to upright palmatum forms with thicker, less-dissected leaves.

**Planting strategy**: Plant in groups of 3-5, always with afternoon shade (east-facing slope or under larger canopy). Deep mulch (10-15cm pine bark) is essential to retain soil moisture. For a 1ha garden, plan **15-25 Japanese maples** total, concentrated in the shaded core zones.

### 1.3 Ground Cover / Moss Substitutes

**Can true moss survive Mediterranean summer?** Short answer: NOT without intensive irrigation. True bryophytes (Polytrichum, Hypnum, Leucobryum) require sustained humidity >70% and consistent moisture. Mediterranean summer (RH 30-40%, no rain for 3 months) is lethal to exposed moss gardens.

**Strategy**: Use moss ONLY in deeply shaded, irrigated micro-zones (north-facing walls, under dense canopy near the pond). Everywhere else, use vascular plant moss-substitutes.

Forms dense moss-like carpets in shade. Dies back in hard frost but regenerates from roots. At -6C it is borderline but at 500-700m with shelter it should survive. **Native Sardinian mosses**: Sardinia hosts Polytrichum juniperinum, Hypnum cupressiforme, and Leucobryum juniperoideum in mountain forests (Gennargentu, Limbara). These are found naturally in holm oak (Quercus ilex) understory at 600-1000m. If you can source local Hypnum cupressiforme, it is the most drought-tolerant European moss, surviving desiccation for weeks and rehydrating after rain. It would be ecologically authentic for a north-facing rock feature.

### 1.4 Bamboo

Fargesia species are clumping (non-invasive) and cold-hardy, BUT they struggle with Mediterranean drought. They are mountain plants from Sichuan (panda habitat) accustomed to humid summers.

| Latin name | Common name | Frost (USDA) | pH range | Water needs | Role | Notes |
|------------|-------------|--------------|----------|-------------|------|-------|
| Fargesia rufa | Green Panda Bamboo | Zone 5 (-29C) | 5.0-7.0 | Moderate-High | Screening, 2-3m | Most sun/heat-tolerant Fargesia. Still needs summer irrigation. |
| Fargesia robusta 'Campbell' | Campbell Bamboo | Zone 7 (-17C) | 5.0-7.0 | Moderate-High | Taller screening, 4-5m | Tolerates more sun than F. nitida. |
| Fargesia murielae 'Jumbo' | Umbrella Bamboo | Zone 5 (-29C) | 5.0-7.0 | Moderate-High | Arching habit, 3-4m | Classic look but dislikes hot sun -- needs shade. |

**Warning on Fargesia in Mediterranean**: Even with irrigation, Fargesia often looks stressed by August. The leaf-curl mechanism that protects from cold also triggers in heat/drought, making them look poorly. **Practical recommendation**: Use Fargesia sparingly (3-5 clumps) in the wettest, most shaded areas (near pond overflow, north-facing). For the bamboo "look" elsewhere, use Nandina domestica (evergreen, drought-tolerant, bamboo-like leaves, red autumn color, no invasion risk).

For a bolder bamboo grove with summer irrigation, consider Phyllostachys nigra (Black Bamboo, Zone 7, running -- MUST use rhizome barrier, 70cm deep HDPE). It handles Mediterranean summers much better than Fargesia and the black culms are stunning in a Japanese garden.

## 2. POND / LAKE DESIGN

### 2.1 Evaporation

At 600-700m in Sardinia, July conditions are approximately:
- Air temperature: 28-32C daytime, 16-20C night
- Relative humidity: 30-50%
- Wind: variable (can be strong at altitude)
- Reference ET0: 5-7 mm/day (T1 -- Montaldo & Oren 2018 measured mean ET 3.4mm/day for Sardinian basins June-Sept, but open water evaporation exceeds vegetated-land ET)

**Open water evaporation estimate**: **6-8 mm/day in July-August** at this altitude. For a 200m2 pond, that is 1,200-1,600 liters/day lost to evaporation in peak summer. Over the 4-month dry season (June-September), total evaporation loss: ~150-200mm, or **30,000-40,000 liters** for a 200m2 pond.

Mitigation:
- Floating plants (waterlilies covering 50-60% of surface) reduce evaporation by 30-40%
- Shading the pond with trees on the south/west side
- Deeper ponds lose proportionally less (less surface area per volume)

### 2.2 EPDM vs Clay Lining

| Factor | EPDM Rubber (1.0-1.5mm) | Puddled/Bentonite Clay |
|--------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| **Cost** | Higher upfront (15-25 EUR/m2 installed) | Lower if clay available locally (5-10 EUR/m2) |
| **Durability** | 40-50 year lifespan, UV-resistant | Permanent if kept hydrated |
| **Mediterranean risk** | None -- inert, stable in heat | **CRITICAL: clay cracks if it dries out**. If pond level drops and clay is exposed to sun, it cracks irreversibly. In a dry Mediterranean summer with potential water shortage, this is a serious risk. |
| **Installation** | Straightforward, can be done by 2-3 people | Requires compaction expertise, thicker layer (30-45cm) |
| **Puncture risk** | Can be punctured by roots/stones (use geotextile underlay) | Self-healing if clay stays hydrated |
| **Appearance** | Black rubber visible at margins (cover with stones) | Natural look, integrates with landscape |
| **Seismic risk** | Flexible, survives minor earth movement | Can crack with ground movement |

**Recommendation for Sardinia**: **EPDM with geotextile underlay**, unequivocally. Sardinia is seismically active (low-moderate), and the summer drying risk makes bentonite clay too dangerous for a permanent koi pond. Use 1.14mm Firestone PondGard or equivalent. Budget: approximately 4,000-6,000 EUR for a 200m2 pond (liner + underlay + edging).

### 2.3 Depth for Koi Survival

At -6C air temperature, ice formation on a pond at 600-700m will be:
- Surface ice: up to 5-10cm thick in coldest spells
- Duration: intermittent, typically a few days at a time (not continuous like northern Europe)

**Koi requirements**:
- Minimum depth: **1.2m** at deepest point (1.5m recommended)
- Below 1m, water temperature stays 4-8C even when surface freezes
- At -6C ambient, a 1.5m deep pond will NOT freeze to the bottom

**Design recommendation**: Main pond area 0.8-1.0m average depth, with a central "koi pit" section 1.5-1.8m deep (at least 4m x 3m). This deep zone serves as winter refuge and summer cool zone (koi stress above 28C water temperature).

### 2.4 Spring-Fed vs Recirculating

If you have a natural spring at the site, this is an enormous advantage.

**Spring-fed pond**:
- Provides continuous fresh oxygenated water
- Maintains cooler temperature in summer
- Reduces or eliminates need for pumped filtration
- Overflow can feed bog garden and irrigation system

**Flow rate needed**: For a 200m2 pond with ~200m3 volume:
- Minimum full turnover: once every 2-3 days = ~1 L/s
- Ideal for koi health: once per day = ~2.3 L/s
- A spring at **1-2 L/s is adequate** for the pond alone. At 2 L/s, you get ~173m3/day, enough for a complete daily turnover.

**Hybrid recommendation**: Use spring water as primary input with a small recirculating pump (low-wattage solar-powered) for the waterfall feature. Install a biological filter (gravel bed, bog garden) through which the spring water passes before entering the main pond. Overflow exits to irrigation.

### 2.5 Algae Management

Without chemicals, in warm Mediterranean conditions:

1. **Biological filtration**: Bog garden filter (10-15% of pond surface area) planted with Iris, Acorus, Caltha, Carex. Spring water passes through gravel + plant roots.
2. **Plant coverage**: Waterlilies covering 50-60% of surface shade out algae.
3. **Barley straw**: 50g/m3 of pond water. Decomposes slowly, releases hydrogen peroxide. Effective, widely used in Europe. Replace every 6 months.
4. **Beneficial bacteria**: Bacillus-based pond treatments (not "chemicals" in the pesticide sense).
5. **UV clarifier**: For green-water algae. Low-energy, effective.
6. **Aeration**: Spring-fed ponds have natural aeration advantage. Add an air stone in the deep koi zone.
7. **Fish stocking density**: Maximum 1 koi per 1-2m3 for low-maintenance water quality.

## 3. IRRIGATION STRATEGY

### 3.1 Micro-Irrigation for Moss Areas

**Mist vs Drip for moss/ground cover**:

| System | Pros | Cons | Best for |
|--------|------|------|----------|
| **Mist nozzles** (20-50 micron droplets) | Humidifies air + soil, mimics fog, moss loves it | High water loss to evaporation in hot/windy conditions (up to 50% loss), requires 3-5 bar pressure, needs clean water (clogs easily) | Enclosed/sheltered moss walls, grotto features, morning-only use |
| **Low-volume micro-sprinklers** (100-200 micron) | Better coverage than drip, less evaporation loss than mist | Still some evaporation loss in wind | Semi-shaded ground cover areas (Soleirolia, Sagina) |
| **Drip irrigation** (2-4 L/h emitters, 20-30cm spacing) | Minimal evaporation loss, precise delivery to root zone | Does not humidify air, can leave dry patches between emitters | Tree root zones, shrub beds, fern beds |

**Recommendation**: Use a **three-zone system**:
1. **Mist zone** (dawn only, 6:00-7:30): Small moss wall and grotto features. Timer-controlled, runs only when wind <5km/h (anemometer switch). ~2 L/m2/day.
2. **Micro-sprinkler zone** (pre-dawn, 5:00-6:00): Soleirolia/Sagina ground cover areas. ~4 L/m2/day in July.
3. **Drip zone** (night, 23:00-01:00): Trees, shrubs, ferns. Variable by plant, 2-8 L/plant/day depending on size.

Rough breakdown for peak month:

### 3.3 Can a Spring at 1-2 L/s Sustain This?

| Spring flow | Daily yield | July need | Surplus/Deficit |
|-------------|-------------|-----------|-----------------|
| 1 L/s | 86,400 L/day | ~14,400 L | **+72,000 L/day surplus** (feeds pond + reserve) |
| 2 L/s | 172,800 L/day | ~14,400 L | **+158,400 L/day surplus** |

**YES, emphatically.** A 1 L/s spring provides **6x the garden's peak daily irrigation need**. The surplus maintains the pond, compensates for evaporation, and could even irrigate additional areas. At 2 L/s you have enormous margin.

**CAVEAT**: Springs in Mediterranean mountains can diminish in late summer. Verify the spring's minimum flow (late August/September) -- not the average. If the spring drops to 0.3 L/s in August (25,920 L/day), you still have a comfortable margin. If it drops below 0.15 L/s, you need a storage cistern.

**Recommendation**: Build a **50-100m3 storage cistern** (buried concrete tank, fed by the spring) as buffer. Even with a reliable spring, this provides 3-7 days of emergency irrigation if something goes wrong.

### 3.4 Rainwater Harvesting

**200m2 roof at 960mm/year**:

Formula: Volume = Roof area x Rainfall x Runoff coefficient
- Runoff coefficient for pitched tile roof: 0.80-0.85
- Volume = 200 x 0.96 x 0.82 = **157m3/year** (~157,000 liters)

Monthly distribution (approximate for this Sardinian location):
| Month | Rainfall mm | Harvest L |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| Jan | 120 | 19,700 |
| Feb | 100 | 16,400 |

The problem: Rain falls when you do not need it (Oct-Mar) and does not fall when you do (Jun-Aug). To capture winter rain for summer use, you would need a **very large cistern** (~100-150m3) to store even a fraction of winter surplus for summer.

**Practical value**: Rainwater from 200m2 is a **supplement** (covers ~35% of one July month's irrigation), not a solution. The spring is your primary water source. But rainwater is valuable for its low pH and absence of minerals -- excellent for acid-loving plants (camellias, azaleas, rhododendrons).

## 4. FIRE RISK MITIGATION

### 4.1 The Montiferru 2021 Context

The July 2021 Montiferru-Planargia fire burned over 13,000 hectares (some estimates up to 20,000) of forest, olive groves, and maquis. It was driven by extreme heat (>40C), very low humidity, and strong winds. This fire demonstrated that at your altitude and in your area, wildfire is not theoretical -- it is a near-certainty on a multi-decade timescale.

### 4.2 The Irrigated Garden as Firebreak

A Japanese garden is inherently more fire-resistant than surrounding maquis/garrigue:
- **Water features**: Pond = natural water reserve for firefighting
- **Irrigated vegetation**: Green, hydrated plants do not burn easily
- **Reduced fuel load**: Raked gravel (karesansui), stone paths, clean understory
- **Low, managed planting**: No accumulated dry brush

**How wide should the irrigated zone be?**

From the Mediterranean Garden Society (T5 -- Trevor Nottle): "A band of [fire-resistant ground cover] roughly **eight metres** wide works well as a firebreak from advancing grass fires."

From CalFire / UC Cooperative Extension (T7): Defensible space zones:
- **Zone 0** (0-1.5m from structures): Non-combustible materials only. Gravel, stone.
- **Zone 1** (1.5-10m): Low, well-irrigated, fire-resistant plants. No trees within 3m of structures.
- **Zone 2** (10-30m): Reduced fuel. Spaced trees, irrigated ground cover.
- **Zone 3** (30-100m): Thinned native vegetation, managed fuel.

**Recommendation for your garden**: The garden itself (1ha) acts as an enormous Zone 1-2. The critical perimeter is the interface with surrounding unmanaged land. Create a **10-15m wide irrigated buffer** around the garden perimeter with:
- Low, green, fire-resistant species (see below)
- No conifers (highly flammable) in the buffer
- Gravel/stone paths as hard firebreaks every 8-10m
- Dedicated irrigation circuit for the perimeter (can be turned to full during fire alert)

### 4.3 Fire-Resistant Plants Compatible with Japanese Aesthetic

From the MGS fires article (T5): "Conifers: Pinus, Cupressus, Thuja, Juniperus... will all burn fiercely." Avoid all conifers in the perimeter zone except trained Pinus sylvestris as isolated specimens well away from boundaries.

**Fire-resistant species that work in a Japanese garden**:

| Latin name | Fire resistance | Japanese garden role |
|------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Myrtus communis | HIGH -- thick, moist leaves | Evergreen hedging, aromatic |
| Arbutus unedo | HIGH -- resists fire, resprouts from root | Structural evergreen tree |
| Cistus spp. | MODERATE -- burns but regenerates fast | Low shrub, rock garden |
| Nerium oleander | HIGH -- very fire resistant | Screening (TOXIC -- keep away from koi pond) |
| Nandina domestica | HIGH -- moist, not volatile | Bamboo substitute |
| Camellia japonica/sasanqua | HIGH -- thick waxy leaves, high moisture | Winter flowering |
| Hydrangea macrophylla | VERY HIGH -- extremely moist tissue | Pond margins |
| Agapanthus | HIGH -- succulent roots resprout | Blue flowers, pond margin |
| Coprosma / Leptospermum | MODERATE | Pruned hedge forms |
| Rosmarinus officinalis | **LOW -- VOLATILE OILS, AVOID** in fire zones | Despite Mediterranean origin, rosemary is FLAMMABLE |
| Lavandula | **LOW -- VOLATILE OILS, AVOID** in fire zones | Same -- essential oils make it a fire vector |

**Specific fire buffer planting plan**:
- Outer ring (10m wide): Myrtus communis low hedge (clipped to 1m) + Arbutus unedo at 8m spacing + gravel mulch (no bark mulch -- bark burns)
- Inner transition: Camellia + Nandina + irrigated Sagina subulata ground cover
- Maintain a 3m gravel/stone "river" (karesansui) as the innermost hard firebreak

### 4.4 Additional Fire Mitigation Measures

1. **Pond as water reserve**: Install a pump (petrol or solar+battery) that can draw from the pond for fire defense. 200m2 at 1m average depth = 200m3 of water. That is 200,000 liters -- enough for significant structure defense.
2. **Roof sprinkler system**: Mount sprinklers on any buildings. Connected to the spring/pond supply.
3. **No combustible mulch**: Use volcanic gravel/pumice instead of bark mulch in the fire buffer zone. Within the garden core, bark mulch is acceptable if irrigated.
4. **Tree canopy separation**: No tree crowns touching in the perimeter zone. Maintain 3m minimum gap between canopies.
5. **Annual cleanup**: Remove all dead/dry material before June. Sardinian regulations require property owners to clear fire hazards.
6. **Access**: Maintain a 4m wide gravel access road for fire trucks.

### Best Seasons to Visit

1. **Late October - mid November**: Autumn maple color at peak + Camellia sasanqua flowering. This is the moment that most closely resembles a Kyoto garden in autumn. Temperature pleasant (15-20C).

2. **April**: Cherry blossom + azalea mass flowering + fresh maple leaves. Classic Japanese spring.

3. **June**: Water garden peak -- waterlilies, iris, hydrangea. Warmth without extreme heat yet.

4. **January-February**: The contemplative winter garden -- camellia japonica, Prunus mume, bare branches, moss. For the aesthete, this is the most Japanese season of all.
