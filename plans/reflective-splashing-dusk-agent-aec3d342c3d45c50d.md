# Montiferru Fire Risk Assessment -- Research Report

## Executive Summary

**The 2021 Montiferru wildfire directly affected San Leonardo de Siete Fuentes.** Multiple news sources (AGI, Corriere, Il Fatto Quotidiano, Protezione Civile Sardegna) explicitly name San Leonardo as an area reached by the fire. The fire originated between Bonarcado and Santu Lussurgiu on July 24, 2021, and the western slope of Montiferru was devastated -- the fire front moved from Santu Lussurgiu through Cuglieri, Sennariolo, and Tresnuraghes all the way to the coast at Porto Alabe. This is a **critical finding** for the site evaluation.

**Epistemic status:** High confidence on the fire perimeter (official Protezione Civile documents + peer-reviewed satellite studies). Medium confidence on recovery status (limited recent Italian-language reporting, some peer-reviewed papers). Medium confidence on comparative fire risk (no single authoritative ranking exists).

**Bottom line for site selection:** Montiferru carries **significantly higher fire risk** than either Gallura (Aggius/Tempio) or Goceano (Bono/Monte Rasu), due to the 2021 event, climate projections, and structural factors. This should be weighted heavily in the ranking.

## 1. Fire Perimeter and Affected Areas

### 1.1 Official perimeter

The fire burned approximately **13,100 hectares** (131 km^2) according to the Nature paper (Pala et al., Scientific Reports 2025), with **80% classified as moderate-high to high severity**. The Protezione Civile Sardegna document (T7) provides the definitive list of affected comuni:

**Area A (main fire -- Montiferru-Planargia):**
- **Bonarcado** (fire origin point)
- **Santu Lussurgiu** (including San Leonardo de Siete Fuentes)
- **Cuglieri** (second most damaged after Sennariolo by % territory)
- **Sennariolo** (most damaged by % territory -- 155 inhabitants fully evacuated)
- **Tresnuraghes** (fire reached Porto Alabe on the coast)
- **Scano di Montiferro** (400 people evacuated)
- **Flussio, Tinnura, Magomadas, Suni, Sagama** (also affected)
- **Sindia** (marginally)

Source: Protezione Civile Sardegna, 28/07/2021 official bulletin; Regione Sardegna OCDPC document (2022).

### 1.2 San Leonardo de Siete Fuentes -- CONFIRMED AFFECTED

Multiple independent sources confirm San Leonardo was directly in the fire path:

1. **AGI (29/07/2021):** "Un nuovo incendio e divampato nell'Oristanese sui monti di Santu Lussurgiu [...] San Leonardo de Siete Fuentes. Nubi di fumo alte sono [visibili]"

2. **Il Fatto Quotidiano (25/07/2021):** "San Leonardo -- dove si trovano le fonti di Siete Fuentes e un piccolo borgo turistico -- e il Monte Sant'Antonio"

3. **Sky TG24 (25/07/2021):** Identical reporting -- San Leonardo explicitly named as affected area.

4. **Corriere della Sera (25/07/2021):** "Da Santulussurgiu ai boschi di Badde Urbara, bruciati ulivi millenari, a San Leonardo di Siete Fuentes fino ai territori di Sindia e Macomer."

5. **Regione Sardegna OCDPC document:** "Il Comune di Santu Lussurgiu ha svolto le suddette attivita sia nel centro abitato che nella **frazione di San Leonardo de Siete Fuentes**, in cui normalmente [ci sono attivita]"

6. **ISSLA ebook (March 2026):** "San Leonardo di Siete Fuentes (dopo il bivio per Santu Lussurgiu verso [...]) Area di avvio dell'incendio tra Bonarcado e Santu Lussurgiu"

7. **Comitato Tutela San Leonardo de Siete Fuentes:** A dedicated committee was formed post-fire specifically for the protection and recovery of San Leonardo (reported by Youtg.net and CronacheNuoresi, November 2021).

**Conclusion:** San Leonardo de Siete Fuentes was unambiguously within the fire perimeter. The SP19/SP20 road corridor from Santu Lussurgiu to San Leonardo passed through burned territory. The fire originated in the area *between Bonarcado and Santu Lussurgiu*, which is essentially the eastern approach to San Leonardo.

### 1.3 Western vs. Eastern slope

The fire's trajectory was:
1. **Origin:** between Bonarcado and Santu Lussurgiu (eastern/southeastern slope)
2. **Spread:** driven by scirocco (SE wind) then libeccio (SW wind), the fire moved **west and northwest** through the Montiferru massif
3. **Path:** Santu Lussurgiu -> San Leonardo -> Cuglieri -> Sennariolo -> Tresnuraghes -> Porto Alabe (coast)

Both slopes were heavily affected. The fire traversed the entire massif from east to west. The **western slope** (facing the sea, around Cuglieri and Tresnuraghes) was devastated. The **eastern slope** (around Santu Lussurgiu and Bonarcado) was the origin point and was also severely burned, including the Badde Urbara forests and millennial olive groves.

The **northeast** sector toward Abbasanta/Ghilarza was less affected -- the fire moved primarily west and northwest rather than east. However, the Santu Lussurgiu territory, which borders the Abbasanta plateau, was heavily hit.

### 1.4 Parts of Santu Lussurgiu territory spared

Based on the fire trajectory and news reports:
- The **town center of Santu Lussurgiu** was not destroyed but was "surrounded by flames" (Wikipedia source, confirmed by multiple Italian reports)
- The **eastern outskirts toward Abbasanta** likely suffered less damage, as the fire moved westward
- The **northern areas toward San Leonardo were burned** -- this is the area of interest
- **Badde Urbara** (forest area) was explicitly mentioned as burned with millennial olive trees destroyed

## 2. Recovery Status (5 years post-fire, as of March 2026)

### 2.1 Vegetation recovery -- Scientific evidence

**Paper 1: Rossetti et al. (2024), "Post-Fire Vegetation (Non-)Recovery across the Edges of a Wildfire", Fire journal (MDPI)**
- Used satellite imagery (dNBR) to assess 1-year post-fire recovery
- Found that recovery was **heterogeneous** -- some areas showed recovery, others showed *non-recovery* (the title is telling)
- Conifer afforestation areas and areas at higher elevations showed the worst recovery
- Native Mediterranean maquis showed better resprouting capacity

**Paper 2: Calderisi et al. (2025), "Relationship between post-fire vegetation recovery and soil temperature in the mediterranean forest", Fire journal (MDPI)**
- Studied the upper section of the Montiferru massif (768 ha)
- Monitored soil temperature in 2022, 2023, and 2024
- The area studied was previously occupied by **conifer afforestation** -- exactly the type of plantation around San Leonardo
- Recovery of conifer plantations is known to be much slower than native maquis

**Paper 3: Esposito et al. (2025), "Response of the Sentinel-1 radar backscattering to an extreme wildfire event", Science of Remote Sensing (Elsevier)**
- Key finding: "both vegetation and soil conditions appear to have recovered to pre-fire levels" for some areas
- BUT this was primarily for low-lying vegetation (maquis)
- The Montiferru volcanic complex showed mixed results
- Post-fire hazards included soil erosion, landslides, debris flows, and floods

**Paper 4: Pala et al. (2025), "Sediment generation through thermal spalling during the 2021 Montiferru-Planargia wildfire", Scientific Reports (Nature)**
- Found significant post-fire sediment generation and debris flows
- Field surveys in August 2022 showed the pre-fire soil surface was "still visible" in some areas (minimal vegetation recovery after 1 year)
- Thermal spalling created new sediment sources not present before the fire
- Afforestation terraces were damaged

### 2.2 Reforestation programs

- **Mosaico Verde** (NGO, 2023 report): Mentioned restoration projects in Montiferru using exclusively native species (arboree autoctone), mixed with shrubs
- **Comitato Tutela San Leonardo de Siete Fuentes:** Formed specifically for post-fire advocacy and recovery of the San Leonardo area
- **Coldiretti Oristano (February 2026):** **55 months after the fire, agricultural businesses still have NOT received the EUR 40 million in promised ristori (compensation)**. Cuglieri is the second most damaged comune after Sennariolo. The article describes ongoing economic collapse in the affected areas.
- **VIA proceedings (October 2023):** Environmental Impact Assessment document references the 2021 fire and mentions San Leonardo as an area of "importance for local identity"

**Key finding:** Recovery is slow. 4.5 years post-fire, agricultural compensation has not arrived. The vegetation recovery is mixed -- maquis resprouting well, but conifer plantations and olive groves (which are what characterize the San Leonardo area) are recovering very slowly or not at all.

### 2.3 Secondary fire risk

The post-fire landscape has **changed the fire risk profile**:
- Dead standing trees and uncleared brush increase fuel load in the short term (2-5 years)
- In the medium term (5-15 years), if maquis regrows without management, it creates dense, fire-prone vegetation
- Conifer plantations, if replanted, take 15-20 years to establish and are highly flammable
- The Wikipedia article states: "It will take at least 15 years to rebuild the woods and the Mediterranean trees destroyed by the flames"
- The previous major fire in Montiferru was in **August 1994** (27 years before 2021)

### 2.4 Soil condition

- Pala et al. (2025) found thermal spalling damaged rock surfaces, creating new sediment sources
- Post-fire soil erosion is a documented problem -- first rainstorms triggered debris flows that carried sediments and ash to the coastline (Esposito et al., 2025)
- Soil is still viable for planting but degraded in high-severity burn areas
- Areas with volcanic soils (basalt) are more resilient; areas with limestone less so

## 3. Fire Risk Assessment for the Future

### 3.1 Cause of the 2021 fire

**Organized arson** -- per Wikipedia (T7, citing Italian judicial sources): "The majority of the wildfires were the result of organised arson, with criminal investigations into the fires ongoing."

The fire started between Bonarcado and Santu Lussurgiu on July 24, 2021. Conditions that enabled the catastrophic spread:
- **Extreme fire danger forecast** issued on July 23 by the CFD (Centro Funzionale Decentrato)
- **Scirocco wind** (hot, dry SE wind) then shifting to **libeccio** (SW wind)
- **High temperatures** sustained for days
- **Low humidity**
- The Protezione Civile had issued a **preallarme** (pre-alarm) for six western and central-northern zones of Sardinia

### 3.2 Historical fire frequency in Montiferru

- **1994:** Previous major fire in Montiferru (per Wikipedia: "Almost 27 years have passed since the last wildfire in Montiferru was in August 1994")
- **2021:** The catastrophic event -- 13,100 hectares
- **Recurrence interval:** roughly 25-30 years for catastrophic events, though smaller fires occur more frequently
- The ISSLA ebook (March 2026) provides a broader history of fires in Sardinia, mentioning Montiferru in the context of the "breve storia degli incendi in Sardegna"

### 3.3 Eastern slope fire risk (San Leonardo area)

The eastern slope (where San Leonardo sits at 684m) has specific risk factors:
- **Origin point:** The 2021 fire literally started here (between Bonarcado and Santu Lussurgiu)
- **Vegetation type:** Mix of conifer plantations (highly flammable), olive groves, and maquis
- **Altitude:** 684m -- moderate altitude, within the zone of maximum fire severity in 2021
- **Wind exposure:** Scirocco winds from the SE directly impact this slope
- **Topography:** The road corridor (SP19/SP20) through the valley toward San Leonardo channels fire

The western slope is more exposed to **libeccio** (SW wind) but the eastern slope is the typical **ignition zone** because it faces the drier interior. In the 2021 event, the eastern slope was where the fire started and then the wind carried it across the mountain.

### 3.4 Climate change projections

Multiple sources confirm fire risk in Sardinia is **increasing significantly**:

1. **IPCC AR6 WG2 CCP4 (Mediterranean Region):** The Mediterranean is identified as a "climate change hotspot" with projected increases in climate hazards, high regional vulnerability and exposure.

2. **CMCC Foundation (Italy):** "In Italy, an increase in fire risk of more than 20% is projected in all climate scenarios."

3. **Bacciu et al. (2021, Sustainability):** Specifically studied Mediterranean islands' fire risk under climate change. Under RCP8.5 scenario, **Sardinia and Sicily move to a higher risk class**.

4. **Pellizzaro et al. (2010, Forest Fire Research):** Estimated effects of future climate on duration of fire danger season **specifically in Sardinia** -- finding a general increase in fire danger.

5. **Lestienne et al. (2022, Climatic Change):** For Corsica (very similar climate to Sardinia): "higher fire hazard intensity after 2050 than during the reference period" under both RCP scenarios.

6. **Marquez Torres et al. (2023, NHESS):** Sicily fire risk model projects a "19% increase for high fire probabilities in 2050" under high-emission scenarios.

7. **ArcGIS StoryMaps (2025):** "Fire Risk Reduction Strategies in Sardinia" -- notes WUI (Wildland-Urban Interface) projections showing important changes.

**Summary:** Fire risk in Sardinia is projected to increase by 20-40% by 2050, with longer fire seasons and more extreme events. This applies to ALL candidate areas, but post-fire Montiferru is especially vulnerable.

## 4. Comparison with Other Candidate Areas

### 4.1 Gallura (Aggius / Tempio Pausania / Limbara)

**Major fire history:**
- **28 July 1983 -- Curraggia fire:** The most catastrophic fire in Sardinian history. Nine people died. The fire burned near Tempio Pausania, in the comuni of Bortigiadas and Aggius. This was a watershed event that led to major reforms in Italian wildfire management.
  - Source: ANSA (2021), Sassari Notizie (2024), multiple historical accounts
  - The 40th anniversary was commemorated in 2023

- **1 August 1989:** Fire between Porto San Paolo, Porto Istana, Costa Dorata, Monte Pedrosu, San Teodoro and Loiri (coastal Gallura, not the interior highlands)
  - Source: Camera dei Deputati PDL 1906

- **16 August 2021:** Further wildfires in Gallura during the same fire season as Montiferru (per Wikipedia article)

**Risk assessment for Aggius/Tempio/Limbara:**
- The Curraggia tragedy demonstrates that the area IS fire-prone
- However, Aggius is at **500-600m altitude** in granite landscape
- Granite terrain with sughera (cork oak) forests -- cork oaks are **fire-adapted** and resprout well
- The interior highland Gallura (around Limbara, 1300m+) has a different microclimate: more precipitation, cooler temperatures, denser forests
- The Gallura fire history is dominated by the coastal and low-altitude areas, not the montane interior around Limbara
- **Infeagallura publication:** "La Sardegna e la Gallura in particolare sono da sempre interessate da incendi: il fuoco e stato uno dei fattori piu rilevanti nel determinare la storia [della vegetazione]"
- This is honest: Gallura IS fire-prone, but the specific risk depends on microsite

### 4.2 Goceano (Bono / Monte Rasu)

**Fire history:**
- I found **no major catastrophic fire events** in the Goceano/Monte Rasu area in my research
- The area appears in forestry documents but NOT in fire disaster databases
- Monte Rasu (1258m) is the highest peak of the Goceano
- The Foresta di Fiorentini (comuni di Anela, Bultei, Bono) is one of the largest and best-managed forests in Sardinia
- The area has a wetter, cooler microclimate than Montiferru or coastal Gallura
- Predominantly deciduous forests (chestnut, oak) rather than fire-prone conifers or maquis
- The Piano Forestale Ambientale Regionale (2008) describes Monte Rasu and the Goceano forests without flagging major fire concerns

**Risk assessment for Bono/Monte Rasu:**
- Lowest fire risk among the three candidates based on available evidence
- Higher altitude (800-1258m), wetter climate, deciduous forests
- No major fire disasters in the historical record
- The Franciscan presence at Monte Rasu since the 1200s, with surviving chestnuts, suggests long-term forest stability

### 4.3 Comparative ranking -- Fire Risk (LOWEST to HIGHEST)

| Rank | Area | Fire Risk | Rationale |
|------|------|-----------|-----------|
| 1 (LOWEST) | **Goceano (Bono/Monte Rasu)** | LOW | No major fire history, highest altitude, wettest climate, deciduous forests, no conifers |
| 2 | **Gallura (Aggius/Tempio/Limbara)** | MEDIUM | Curraggia 1983 was catastrophic but in low-altitude area; highland Limbara is wetter; granite + cork oak = fire-adapted; but area IS historically fire-prone |
| 3 (HIGHEST) | **Montiferru (Santu Lussurgiu/San Leonardo)** | HIGH-VERY HIGH | Catastrophic 2021 fire directly hit the area; fire origin point; post-fire landscape is degraded; 25-30 year recurrence; western Sardinia is drier; agricultural compensation still pending |

## 5. Critical Implications for Site Selection

### 5.1 San Leonardo de Siete Fuentes -- specific risks

1. **The 2021 fire directly hit this location.** This is not a nearby event -- it burned through San Leonardo.
2. **The fire originated in the immediate vicinity** (between Bonarcado and Santu Lussurgiu, a few km away).
3. **Vegetation recovery at 5 years is mixed** -- scientific papers show conifer plantations (present around San Leonardo) recover poorly.
4. **Agricultural economy has collapsed** -- 55 months post-fire, no compensation received (Coldiretti, Feb 2026).
5. **Next 10-15 years are the highest-risk period** -- post-fire landscapes with dead material, regrowing maquis, and potential new plantations create high fuel loads.
6. **Climate change will increase fire risk** -- CMCC projects >20% increase for all Italian regions.
7. **The arson cause means it can happen again** -- organized arson is a systemic problem in Sardinia, not a one-time event.

### 5.2 Is this a dealbreaker?

**Assessment: This is a serious negative factor, but not necessarily a dealbreaker** -- depending on the purpose of the site. Considerations:

- If the plan involves **building permanent structures or planting trees for long-term investment**, the fire risk is a major concern. Any structure or plantation could be destroyed in the next 25-30 year fire cycle.
- If the plan involves **land with existing structures that survived 2021**, those structures have been "fire-tested" and the immediate post-fire period may actually see lower vegetation fuel loads.
- **Insurance costs** will be significantly higher in a documented fire zone.
- **Property values** may be lower (opportunity), but also because the risk is priced in.
- The community formation of the Comitato Tutela San Leonardo suggests local engagement in recovery.

### 5.3 Recommendation

**Goceano (Bono/Monte Rasu) should be strongly considered as an alternative if fire risk is a significant factor.** It has:
- No catastrophic fire history
- Wetter, higher altitude climate
- Deciduous forests (less fire-prone than conifer or maquis)
- Still within central Sardinia (reasonably accessible)
- A beautiful, forested mountainous landscape

**Gallura (Aggius/Tempio)** is intermediate -- the Curraggia history is concerning, but the highland areas around Limbara are significantly less risky than Montiferru.
